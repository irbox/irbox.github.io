1. A forty-five-year-old male is brought to the casualty with the complaint of sudden onset of severe abdominal pain and vomiting. He had taken a few analgesic tablets for musculoskeletal pain earlier. On examination, he is dehydrated, PR 102/minute, his abdomen is rigid and liver dullness is obliterated. Discuss the probable aetiology, evaluation and treatment options for his condition.

Answer:
Probable aetiology includes acute pancreatitis, perforated peptic ulcer, or biliary colic. Evaluation involves imaging studies such as ultrasound or CT scan. Treatment options include fluid resuscitation, pain management, and possibly surgery depending on diagnosis.

or

Define surgical site infections, risk factors for development of SSI, and its prevention and management.

Answer:
Surgical site infections (SSI) are infections occurring at the site of a surgical incision. Risk factors include patient-related factors (diabetes, obesity) and procedural factors (contamination during surgery). Prevention includes proper aseptic technique and antibiotic prophylaxis. Management includes wound care, drainage, and antibiotics if necessary.

2. A fifty-five-year-old male attends the surgical OPD with the complaints of abdominal pain, loss of appetite, progressive jaundice and itching. Discuss the probable causes, evaluation and treatment options for his condition.

Answer:
Probable causes include pancreatic cancer, cholangiocarcinoma, or choledocholithiasis. Evaluation could involve liver function tests, imaging (ultrasound, CT scan), and possibly endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP). Treatment options may include surgical intervention or palliative care depending on the underlying cause.

or

Describe etiopathogenesis, clinical features, and management of oral malignancies.

Answer:
Etiopathogenesis involves risk factors such as tobacco and alcohol use, HPV infection, and poor oral hygiene. Clinical features include non-healing ulcers, lumps in the mouth, and difficulty swallowing. Management includes surgical excision, potentially followed by radiation or chemotherapy.

3. Gastric outlet obstruction

Answer:
Gastric outlet obstruction is a condition where the passage from the stomach to the duodenum is blocked, often due to peptic ulcers or malignancy. Symptoms include vomiting and weight loss. Management typically involves surgical intervention to relieve the obstruction.

or

States of TB Lymphadenitis

Answer:
TB lymphadenitis is an extrapulmonary manifestation of tuberculosis where lymph nodes become infected. Common symptoms include swelling and pain in lymph nodes. Treatment involves antitubercular therapy for at least six months.

Acute appendicitis

Answer:
Acute appendicitis is the inflammation of the appendix, presenting with abdominal pain, nausea, and fever. Diagnosis is made through clinical examination and imaging. Treatment involves surgical removal of the appendix (appendectomy).

5. Sigmoid volvulus

Answer:
Sigmoid volvulus is twisting of the sigmoid colon leading to obstruction. Symptoms include abdominal pain and distension. Management typically involves endoscopy for detorsion and possibly surgical resection if necrosis is present.

or

Complications after Thyroid surgery

Answer:
Complications can include hypoparathyroidism, recurrent laryngeal nerve injury, and hematoma formation. Prevention involves meticulous surgical technique and postoperative monitoring.

6. Haemorrhoids

Answer:
Haemorrhoids are swollen veins in the lower rectum or anus. They can be internal or external, with symptoms including bleeding and discomfort. Management includes dietary modifications, topical treatments, and possibly surgical intervention in severe cases.

or

Ranula

Answer:
A ranula is a type of mucocele that appears in the floor of the mouth, usually due to salivary gland obstruction. Treatment often includes surgical removal of the cyst.

7. Pelvic abscess

Answer:
Pelvic abscess is a collection of pus in the pelvic cavity, often resulting from infection. Symptoms include fever, abdominal pain, and malaise. Management typically involves drainage and antibiotics.

or

Trophic Ulcer

Answer:
Trophic ulcers are skin ulcers resulting from inadequate blood supply. Management includes improving blood flow and wound care.

8. Mesenteric Ischaemia

Answer:
Mesenteric ischaemia is a condition caused by insufficient blood flow to the intestines, often due to arterial occlusion. Symptoms include severe abdominal pain and diarrhea. Immediate treatment is crucial and may involve surgery.

or

Parklands Formula

Answer:
The Parkland formula is used to estimate fluid replacement needs in burn patients, calculated as 4 mL x % TBSA burned x body weight in kg over the first 24 hours.

9. Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease

Answer:
Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GERD) is a chronic condition where stomach acid flows back into the esophagus, causing symptoms like heartburn. Management includes lifestyle changes and medications such as proton pump inhibitors.

or

Management of Basal Cell carcinoma

Answer:
Basal cell carcinoma is primarily treated by surgical excision. Other options include Mohs micrographic surgery, cryotherapy, and topical chemotherapy for superficial forms.

10. Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy

Answer:
Laparoscopic cholecystectomy is a minimally invasive surgical procedure to remove the gallbladder. Benefits include shorter recovery time and reduced postoperative pain compared to open surgery.

or

Complications of hernia

Answer:
Complications of hernias may include incarceration, strangulation, and bowel obstruction. Management typically requires surgical intervention to repair the hernia and mitigate symptoms.
