LONG ESSAYS 2 x 10 = 20 Marks

1. Etiology, clinical features, investigations and management of thrombo angitis.
Answer: Answer: Thromboangiitis obliterans, or Buerger's disease, is characterized by the inflammation and thrombosis of small- and medium-sized blood vessels. Clinical features include pain in the affected areas, ulcers, and gangrene. Investigations involve angiography, Doppler studies, and biopsy. Management includes smoking cessation, medications to improve blood flow, and in severe cases, surgical options.

2. Etiopathogenesis, clinical features, diagnosis and management of tuberculous lymphadenitis.
Answer: Answer: Tuberculous lymphadenitis is caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis. Clinical features include swollen lymph nodes, fever, night sweats, and weight loss. Diagnosis is through fine needle aspiration cytology, biopsy, and culture. Management typically involves antituberculous therapy for 6 to 12 months.

SHORT ESSAYS: 3 x 5 = 15 Marks

3. Rodent ulcer.
Answer: Answer: Rodent ulcer, or basal cell carcinoma, is a skin cancer that common presents as a non-healing sore or a pearly nodule. Treatment includes surgical excision or Mohs micrographic surgery.

4. Electrolyte abnormalities in congenital hypertrophic pyloric stenosis.
Answer: Answer: In congenital hypertrophic pyloric stenosis, patients often present with hypochloremic, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis due to prolonged vomiting. Management focuses on rehydration and correcting electrolyte imbalances before surgical intervention.

5. Cystic hygroma.
Answer: Answer: Cystic hygroma is a benign lymphatic malformation typically found in the neck or axilla of children. It presents as a soft, compressible mass. Treatment usually involves surgical excision or sclerotherapy.

SHORT ANSWERS 5 x 3 = 15 Marks

6. Plunging ranula.
Answer: Answer: A plunging ranula is a type of ranula that extends from the sublingual space into the neck. It usually presents as a swelling in the floor of the mouth or neck and may require surgical intervention for resolution.

7. Speckled leukoplakia.
Answer: Answer: Speckled leukoplakia is characterized by white patches on the mucous membranes that may have a red background, often associated with potential malignancy. Biopsy is necessary for diagnosis and appropriate management.

8. Sternomastoid tumour.
Answer: Answer: Sternomastoid tumor, or congenital muscular torticollis, is a condition where the sternocleidomastoid muscle is shortened, causing the head to tilt to one side. Treatment involves physiotherapy, and in persistent cases, surgical release may be needed.

9. Cancrum oris.
Answer: Answer: Cancrum oris, or noma, is a rapidly progressive gangrenous process of the oral cavity, often following malnutrition and infections. Management includes aggressive antibiotic therapy and surgical intervention as needed.

10. Adson’s test.
Answer: Answer: Adson’s test is a clinical maneuver used to assess thoracic outlet syndrome. It involves palpating the radial pulse while the patient extends their neck and turns their head towards the tested side. A diminished pulse suggests compression of the neurovascular bundle.
