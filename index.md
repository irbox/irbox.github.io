**Surgery Paper 1 - RGUHS RS4 CBME Focus Notes**

**8 marks - Breast [as 10m] [as 1m]**

*   **Applied Anatomy & Investigations:**
    *   **Anatomy:** Modified sweat gland, 15-20 lobes -> ducts -> lactiferous sinus -> nipple. Cooper's ligaments (suspensory). Blood supply: Int mammary, Lat thoracic, Intercostals. Lymphatics: Axillary (Levels I, II, III relative to pec minor), Internal Mammary, Supraclavicular.
    *   **Investigations:** **Triple Assessment** is KEY.
        1.  **Clinical Examination:** Inspection (symmetry, skin changes, nipple), Palpation (lump characteristics - site, size, shape, surface, consistency, mobility, fixation; axilla, supra/infraclavicular nodes).
        2.  **Imaging:**
            *   Mammography: >40 yrs (or <40 with high suspicion/family hx). Signs: density, microcalcifications (malignant type: pleomorphic, linear, branching), spiculation, architectural distortion. BIRADS scoring.
            *   Ultrasound (USG): <40 yrs, dense breasts, differentiate cystic/solid. Signs: shape (taller > wider = malignant), margins (irregular, spiculated), echogenicity (hypoechoic), posterior acoustic shadowing.
            *   MRI: High-risk screening, problem-solving, staging.
        3.  **Pathology:**
            *   FNAC (Fine Needle Aspiration Cytology): Differentiates cystic/solid, cytology grading (C1-C5). Less reliable than core.
            *   Core Biopsy (Tru-Cut): **Gold standard.** Histology, grading, receptor status (ER, PR, HER2).
            *   Excision Biopsy: For diagnosis if core biopsy inconclusive or for benign lesion removal.

*   **Benign Breast Disease (including infections):**
    *   **Etiopathogenesis:** Aberrations of Normal Development and Involution (ANDI) - spectrum based on hormonal influence. Infections: Lactational (Staph aureus via cracked nipple), Non-lactational (periductal mastitis).
    *   **Clinical Features:** Pain (cyclical/non-cyclical mastalgia), Lumpiness (fibroadenosis), Nipple discharge (duct ectasia, papilloma), Infection (pain, redness, swelling, fever - mastitis; fluctuant mass - abscess).
    *   **Principles of Management:**
        *   ANDI: Reassurance, analgesia (NSAIDs, primrose oil), supportive bra. Danazol/Tamoxifen if severe.
        *   Infections: Antibiotics (Flucloxacillin), continue breastfeeding/pump, USG +/- aspiration/drainage for abscess (Hilton's method inapplicable). Duct ectasia: Conservative, Hadfield's operation (total duct excision) if recurrent.

*   **Benign & Malignant Tumours:**
    *   **Etiopathogenesis:** Benign: Fibroadenoma (commonest, young women, 'breast mouse'), Phyllodes (fibroepithelial, potential for malignancy). Malignant: Risk factors (Age, Female, Genetics - BRCA1/2, Family Hx, Hormonal - early menarche, late menopause, nulliparity, HRT, OCPs, Radiation, Obesity, Alcohol). Pathogenesis: Genetic mutations (oncogenes, tumor suppressor genes).
    *   **Clinical Features:** Benign: Fibroadenoma (firm, mobile, well-defined), Phyllodes (large, rapid growth). Malignant: Hard, irregular, painless lump (MC), skin tethering/dimpling, peau d'orange, nipple retraction/discharge (bloody), axillary lymphadenopathy. Paget's disease (eczema-like nipple changes).
    *   **Investigations:** Triple Assessment (as above). Staging for Malignant (TNM): CT Chest/Abdo/Pelvis, Bone Scan or PET-CT.
    *   **Principles of Treatment:**
        *   **Benign:** Fibroadenoma (observe if small, excise if >3cm/patient preference), Phyllodes (Wide local excision due to recurrence risk).
        *   **Malignant:** Multidisciplinary Team (MDT) approach.
            *   **Surgery:** Breast Conserving Surgery (BCS - lumpectomy/WLE + radiotherapy) OR Mastectomy (Simple, Modified Radical - MRM). Axillary Staging: Sentinel Lymph Node Biopsy (SLNB) or Axillary Lymph Node Dissection (ALND). Reconstruction options.
            *   **Adjuvant Therapy:** Based on stage, grade, receptor status.
                *   Radiotherapy: After BCS, post-mastectomy if high risk.
                *   Chemotherapy: Neoadjuvant (downstaging) or Adjuvant (systemic control). (e.g., FAC, TAC, CMF regimes).
                *   Hormonal Therapy: If ER/PR positive (Tamoxifen, Aromatase inhibitors).
                *   Targeted Therapy: If HER2 positive (Trastuzumab/Herceptin).

**6 marks - Thyroid and Parathyroid Glands [as 10m] [as 5m]**

*   **Thyroid Anatomy & Physiology:**
    *   **Anatomy:** Butterfly shape, 2 lobes + isthmus at C5-T1 level. Pretracheal fascia capsule. Blood: Sup Thyroid A (from ECA), Inf Thyroid A (from thyrocervical trunk), +/- Thyroid Ima A. Veins follow arteries. Nerves: Recurrent Laryngeal N (RLN - posterior, risk during Inf Thyroid A ligation), External branch of Superior Laryngeal N (SLN - risk during Sup Thyroid A ligation). Lymphatics: Prelaryngeal, pretracheal, paratracheal, deep cervical.
    *   **Physiology:** Synthesis (Iodine trapping, organification, coupling), Storage (colloid), Release of T3 (active), T4 (prohormone). Controlled by TSH (pituitary) via TRH (hypothalamus). Calcitonin from parafollicular C-cells (lowers Ca2+).

*   **Etiopathogenesis of Thyroid Swellings (Goitre):**
    *   **Simple Goitre:** Iodine deficiency (endemic), dyshormonogenesis, goitrogens. Diffuse -> Multinodular (MNG). Non-toxic.
    *   **Toxic Goitre:** Graves' disease (autoimmune, TSH-R Ab), Toxic MNG (Plummer's disease), Toxic Adenoma. Hyperthyroidism.
    *   **Neoplastic:** Benign (Follicular adenoma), Malignant (Papillary, Follicular, Medullary, Anaplastic).
    *   **Inflammatory:** Hashimoto's (autoimmune, hypothyroidism), De Quervain's (viral, transient hyper -> hypo), Riedel's (fibrous, rare).

*   **Thyroid Cancer:**
    *   **Clinical Features:** Solitary nodule (MC presentation), rapidly enlarging, hard, fixed, cervical lymphadenopathy, hoarseness (RLN), dysphagia/dyspnea. Medullary Ca: Diarrhoea, flushing (calcitonin/other peptides), MEN2 association.
    *   **Classification:**
        *   Papillary (MC, ~80%): Young, F>M, **Lymphatic spread**, Orphan Annie eyes, Psammoma bodies. Good prognosis.
        *   Follicular (~10%): Older, **Hematogenous spread**, requires capsular/vascular invasion to differentiate from adenoma. RAS mutation. Good prognosis if minimally invasive.
        *   Medullary (~5%): C-cells, produces Calcitonin (tumor marker), sporadic or familial (MEN2A/2B, RET mutation). Lymphatic & Hematogenous spread. Moderate prognosis. Amyloid stroma.
        *   Anaplastic (<2%): Elderly, very aggressive, rapid growth, poor prognosis.
        *   Lymphoma: Usually background of Hashimoto's.
    *   **Principles of Management:** MDT approach.
        *   Surgery: Total Thyroidectomy (standard) +/- Central/Lateral Neck Dissection (if LN involved/prophylactic in Medullary). Hemi-thyroidectomy (lobectomy) for small, low-risk papillary/follicular.
        *   Radioactive Iodine (RAI) Ablation: For differentiated thyroid Ca (Papillary/Follicular) post-operatively to destroy remnants/mets.
        *   TSH Suppression: Levothyroxine to keep TSH low (reduces recurrence risk for differentiated Ca).
        *   External Beam Radiotherapy (EBRT): Anaplastic, unresectable/residual disease.
        *   Chemotherapy/Targeted Therapy: Anaplastic, advanced/metastatic disease.

*   **Parathyroid Anatomy:** Usually 4 glands, posterior surface of thyroid lobes (variable location). Sup glands (from 4th pouch), Inf glands (from 3rd pouch, migrate with thymus). Blood supply mainly from Inf Thyroid A.

*   **Hypo- & Hyperparathyroidism:**
    *   **Hypoparathyroidism:** Causes: Post-surgical (thyroidectomy/parathyroidectomy - MC), autoimmune, DiGeorge syndrome. Features: Hypocalcemia -> neuromuscular excitability (Tetany, paresthesia, cramps, laryngospasm), Chvostek's sign, Trousseau's sign, prolonged QT. Management: Acute (IV Calcium Gluconate), Chronic (Oral Calcium, Active Vit D - Calcitriol).
    *   **Hyperparathyroidism:**
        *   Primary: Adenoma (~85%), Hyperplasia (~15%), Carcinoma (<1%). Features: "Stones, Bones, Abdominal Groans, Psychic Moans" (Renal calculi, Osteitis fibrosa cystica/bone pain, PUD/pancreatitis/constipation, Depression/fatigue). Hypercalcemia, High PTH.
        *   Secondary: Response to chronic hypocalcemia (CRF, Vit D deficiency). High PTH, Low/Normal Ca2+. Bone disease (renal osteodystrophy).
        *   Tertiary: Autonomous PTH secretion after prolonged secondary hyperpara (usually post-renal transplant). High PTH, High Ca2+.
    *   **Principles of Management:**
        *   Primary: Surgery (Parathyroidectomy - focused/minimally invasive if localized via Sestamibi/USG; bilateral neck exploration if not localized/hyperplasia). Medical: Cinacalcet (calcimimetic), Bisphosphonates.
        *   Secondary: Treat underlying cause (Vit D, phosphate binders, manage CRF). Cinacalcet.
        *   Tertiary: Parathyroidectomy.

**6 marks - Peri-operative Care [as 3m] [as 1m][as 1m]**

*   **Principles:** Goal = safe surgery, optimal outcome.
    *   **Pre-operative:**
        *   Assessment: History (co-morbidities, allergies, meds, anaesthetic hx), Exam, Investigations (baseline Hb, U&E, Cr, ECG, CXR, group & save/crossmatch as needed). Risk Stratification (ASA grade).
        *   Optimization: Manage co-morbidities (diabetes, HTN, resp disease), Nutrition, Stop anticoagulants/antiplatelets appropriately.
        *   Consent: Informed, written. Discuss risks/benefits/alternatives.
        *   Preparation: NBM (Nil By Mouth), Bowel prep (if needed), Prophylaxis (Antibiotics - single dose pre-incision; DVT - stockings, LMWH). Site marking.
    *   **Intra-operative:**
        *   WHO Surgical Safety Checklist (Sign in, Time out, Sign out).
        *   Anaesthesia: GA/Regional/Local.
        *   Monitoring: ECG, BP, SpO2, ETCO2, Temp, UO.
        *   Positioning: Prevent nerve injury/pressure sores.
        *   Fluid Management. Asepsis. Hemostasis.
    *   **Post-operative:**
        *   Recovery: Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Pain control.
        *   Ward Care: Monitoring vitals, Fluid balance, Pain management (analgesic ladder), Nausea/vomiting control, DVT/Antibiotic prophylaxis continued if needed, Mobilization, Nutrition advancement.
        *   Wound Care: Monitor for infection/hematoma.
        *   Complication Management: Early recognition & treatment (e.g., hemorrhage, infection, atelectasis, ileus, DVT/PE). Discharge planning.

*   **Shock:** See Shock section below. Pathophysiology (O2 debt, anaerobic metabolism, acidosis, SIRS, MODS).
*   **Wound Healing:** See Wound Healing section below. Phases, factors affecting, types of closure.

Consequences: Hyperglycemia, protein breakdown, Na+/water retention. Aim of care: minimize stress, provide nutrition, control sepsis.

**5 marks - Trauma [as 3m]**

*   **Mass Casualties Principles:** Greatest good for greatest number. Triage (priority setting based on severity & salvageability). Primary Triage (SORT/SIEVE: Dead, Immediate, Urgent, Delayed). Secondary Triage (at casualty clearing station). Command & Control structure. Communication. ATLS principles applied.
*   **Head Injuries Pathophysiology/Mechanism:**
    *   Pathophysio: Primary injury (direct impact, coup/contrecoup, axonal shear). Secondary injury (hypoxia, hypotension, raised ICP, edema, herniation, seizures - potentially preventable). Monroe-Kellie doctrine (fixed intracranial volume: brain + blood + CSF).
    *   Mechanism: Blunt (RTA, falls, assault), Penetrating (GSW, stab). Acceleration/Deceleration/Rotational forces.
*   **Neuro Assessment & GCS:**
    *   Assessment: ABCDE first. AVPU. Pupil size/reactivity. Focal deficits.
    *   **Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS):** Assesses level of consciousness. Score 3-15.
        *   **Eye Opening (E):** Spontaneous (4), To speech (3), To pain (2), None (1).
        *   **Verbal Response (V):** Orientated (5), Confused (4), Inappropriate words (3), Incomprehensible sounds (2), None (1).
        *   **Motor Response (M):** Obeys commands (6), Localises pain (5), Withdraws from pain (4), Flexion to pain (abnormal) (3), Extension to pain (2), None (1).
    *   Severity: Mild (13-15), Moderate (9-12), Severe (3-8).

*   **Head Injury Investigations & Management:**
    *   Investigations: **Non-contrast CT Head** (gold standard) - indications (GCS <15, focal deficit, seizure, vomiting >1, anticoagulated, dangerous mechanism, skull #). Skull X-ray (limited role).
    *   Principles of Management:
        1.  ABCDE (Airway with C-spine control, Breathing - O2, Circulation - maintain BP).
        2.  Neuro Assessment (GCS, pupils).
        3.  Control ICP: Head up 30°, Mannitol, Hypertonic saline, +/- Hyperventilation (briefly), Sedation. Avoid hypoxia/hypotension.
        4.  Seizure prophylaxis (if needed).
        5.  Surgery: Evacuation of hematoma (EDH, SDH, ICH), Decompressive craniectomy (for refractory raised ICP).

*   **Soft Tissue Injuries:**
    *   Clinical Features: Abrasion, Contusion (bruise), Hematoma (collection), Laceration (cut), Avulsion. Assess neurovascular status distal to injury.
    *   Investigations: Usually clinical. X-ray if # suspected. USG for hematoma/collection.
    *   Principles of Management: RICE (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation). Wound cleaning (irrigation), Debridement (remove devitalized tissue/contaminants), Hemostasis, Closure (sutures, staples, steri-strips - consider delayed if contaminated), Dressing, Tetanus prophylaxis. Antibiotics if high risk/infected.

*   **Chest Injuries Pathophysiology:** Blunt (RTA, fall) or Penetrating (stab, GSW). Disruption of chest wall integrity, lung parenchyma, pleura, great vessels, airways, heart. Leads to impaired ventilation (pain, pneumo/hemothorax, flail chest) and/or circulation (tamponade, massive hemothorax, tension pneumo).

*   **Chest Injury Features & Management:**
    *   Clinical Features: Pain, Dyspnea, Tachypnea, Hypoxia, Bruising, Crepitus (surgical emphysema), Deformity (flail), Decreased/absent breath sounds, Tracheal deviation (tension pneumo), Distended neck veins, Hypotension/Shock.
    *   Principles of Management:
        1.  **Primary Survey (ABCDE) & Resuscitation:** High flow O2. Address life-threats immediately:
            *   Airway obstruction -> secure airway.
            *   Tension Pneumothorax -> Needle decompression (2nd ICS MCL) then Chest Drain.
            *   Open Pneumothorax -> 3-sided dressing then Chest Drain.
            *   Massive Hemothorax -> Chest Drain + Volume resus +/- Thoracotomy.
            *   Flail Chest -> Analgesia, O2, +/- PPV.
            *   Cardiac Tamponade -> Pericardiocentesis +/- Thoracotomy.
        2.  **Secondary Survey:** Detailed exam, CXR (erect if possible), FAST scan/eFAST, CT Chest (stable patients).
        3.  **Definitive Care:** Chest drain insertion (5th ICS AAL), Analgesia (vital for breathing), Rib fracture management (conservative), Surgical repair if needed.

**5 marks - Minimally Invasive Surgery (MIS) [as 5m] [as 5m]**

*   **Indications:** Wide range including Cholecystectomy (gold standard), Appendectomy, Hernia repair (TEP/TAPP), Colectomy, Fundoplication, Diagnostic laparoscopy, Adrenalectomy, Nephrectomy, Bariatric surgery etc.
*   **Advantages:**
    *   Reduced post-operative pain.
    *   Smaller incisions -> Better cosmesis.
    *   Shorter hospital stay.
    *   Faster recovery & return to normal activities/work.
    *   Reduced risk of wound infection & incisional hernia.
    *   Better visualization (magnification).
*   **Disadvantages:**
    *   Loss of tactile feedback.
    *   Loss of 3D vision (usually 2D screen).
    *   Steeper learning curve for surgeons.
    *   Higher equipment cost.
    *   Longer operative time initially.
    *   Potential specific complications: Veress/Trocar injury (vessel, bowel), CO2 embolism, Referred shoulder tip pain (phrenic irritation), Subcutaneous emphysema, Hypercarbia/Acidosis.
*   **Principles:** Access (Veress needle/Hasson open technique), Insufflation (CO2 to create pneumoperitoneum ~12-15mmHg), Visualization (Laparoscope/camera), Triangulation (optimal instrument placement), Manipulation (specialized instruments), Extraction.

**5 marks - Nutrition and Fluid Therapy**

*   **Malnutrition Causes & Consequences:**
    *   **Causes:** Reduced intake (anorexia, dysphagia, obstruction, poverty), Increased losses (diarrhoea, vomiting, fistula), Increased requirements (sepsis, trauma, burns, surgery, malignancy), Impaired absorption (IBD, short bowel).
    *   **Consequences:** Impaired wound healing, Increased infection risk (immune dysfunction), Muscle weakness (resp failure, poor mobilization), Apathy/depression, Increased morbidity/mortality, Longer hospital stay.
*   **Fluid & Electrolyte Estimation & Replacement:**
    *   **Estimation:** Clinical assessment (Hx - intake/output, Exam - JVP, pulse, BP, edema, mucous membranes, skin turgor). Urine output monitoring (aim >0.5ml/kg/hr). Monitor U&E, lactate.
    *   **Requirements:** Maintenance ~ 30-35 ml/kg/day water, 1 mmol/kg/day Na+ & K+, 50-100g/day glucose.
    *   **Replacement:** Add ongoing losses (NG aspirate, drains, fistula output, third space loss) + Correct existing deficit. Use Crystalloids (Normal Saline 0.9%, Ringer's Lactate, Hartmann's) for resuscitation/replacement. Colloids (Albumin, Starches - limited use) for specific indications. Dextrose solutions for maintenance/hypoglycemia.
*   **Nutritional Requirements & Support:**
    *   **Requirements:** Basal Energy Expenditure (BEE) - use Harris-Benedict equation, adjust for stress/activity factor. Protein ~1-1.5 g/kg/day (higher if catabolic). Need Macronutrients (Carbs, Fat, Protein) & Micronutrients (Vitamins, Trace elements).
    *   **Nutritional Support Methods:**
        *   **Enteral Nutrition (EN):** Preferred route ("If the gut works, use it"). Via NG tube, NJ tube, PEG (Percutaneous Endoscopic Gastrostomy), Jejunostomy. Standard feeds or specialized formulas.
        *   **Parenteral Nutrition (PN):** IV route if gut non-functional/inaccessible. Via Peripheral line (PPN - short term, lower osmolarity) or Central line (TPN - long term, higher osmolarity). All-in-one bags (glucose, amino acids, lipids, electrolytes, vitamins, trace elements).
    *   **Complications:**
        *   EN: Tube displacement/blockage, Aspiration, Diarrhoea, Cramping.
        *   PN: Line sepsis (major risk), Thrombosis, Refeeding syndrome (Hypo- K+, PO4, Mg++), Hyperglycemia, Electrolyte imbalance, Liver dysfunction (steatosis).

**5 marks - Investigations in a Surgical Patient [as 5m]**

*   **Choosing Investigations:** Based on Hx & Exam. Consider: Purpose (diagnosis, staging, fitness for surgery), Availability, Cost, Invasiveness, Patient factors.
    *   **Biochemical:** FBC (anemia, infection), U&E (renal function, electrolytes), LFTs (liver function, nutrition), Coagulation screen (PT/INR, APTT), Amylase/Lipase (pancreatitis), CRP/ESR (inflammation), Glucose, TFTs, Calcium, PSA etc.
    *   **Microbiological:** Culture & Sensitivity (blood, urine, pus, sputum, tissue), Gram stain, AFB stain, Viral serology.
    *   **Pathological:** Histology (biopsy - core/incisional/excisional, resection specimen), Cytology (FNAC, fluid aspirates). Grading & Staging. Immunohistochemistry (receptors).
    *   **Imaging:**
        *   X-ray: Plain (Chest, Abdomen - perforation/obstruction, KUB - stones, Bone - #), Contrast studies (Barium swallow/meal/enema, IVU, Angiography).
        *   Ultrasound (USG): Solid organs (liver, kidney, spleen), Gallbladder, Appendix, Thyroid, Breast, Vascular (Doppler), FAST scan. Differentiates cystic/solid. Guided biopsy/aspiration.
        *   CT Scan: Detailed cross-sectional anatomy. Trauma, Malignancy staging, Abscess, Vascular (CTA). With/without contrast.
        *   MRI: Superior soft tissue detail. Neuro, MSK, Pelvis, Liver (MRCP). With/without contrast (Gadolinium).
        *   Nuclear Medicine: Bone scan (mets), Thyroid scan, HIDA scan (biliary), PET scan (metabolic activity - oncology).
    *   **Endoscopy:** Gastroscopy, Colonoscopy, Sigmoidoscopy, ERCP, Bronchoscopy, Cystoscopy. Diagnostic & Therapeutic. Biopsy capability.
*   **Early Cancer Detection & MDT Approach:**
    *   **Biological Basis:** Understanding tumor markers (AFP, CEA, CA19-9, PSA, Calcitonin - limited screening value, better for monitoring), Genetic predisposition (BRCA, HNPCC, FAP - screening high-risk groups), Pre-malignant conditions (Barrett's, Leukoplakia, Polyps).
    *   **Early Detection:** Screening programs (Breast - Mammo, Cervical - Pap, Colorectal - FOBT/Colonoscopy). Patient education (warning signs). Prompt investigation of suspicious symptoms.
    *   **MDT Approach:** Essential for cancer management. Core team (Surgeon, Oncologist - Medical/Radiation, Radiologist, Pathologist, Specialist Nurse). Input from Palliative care, Dietician, Physio etc. Regular meetings to discuss diagnosis, staging, treatment plan (surgery, chemo, radio, targeted, hormonal), follow-up. Improves outcomes.

**5 marks - Skin Lesions (Ulcer, Sinus, Fistula, Malignancies) [as 3m]**

*   **Ulcer:** Break in continuity of covering epithelium (skin/mucous membrane).
    *   Describe: Site, Size, Shape, Edge (Sloping - healing, Punched out - trophic/syphilitic, Undermined - TB, Rolled/Everted - Malignant), Floor (granulation tissue, slough, visible structures), Base (tissue underneath - mobility, induration), Discharge (serous, purulent, bloody), Surrounding skin, Regional LNs.
    *   Types: Traumatic, Venous, Arterial, Neuropathic (Trophic), Diabetic, Pressure, Malignant (SCC, BCC, Melanoma), Infective (TB, Syphilis).
    *   Management: Treat cause, Debridement, Dressings (promote granulation/epithelialization - VAC), +/- Skin graft/flap. Biopsy if suspicious.
*   **Sinus:** Blind tract lined by granulation tissue, connecting surface epithelium to deeper cavity/focus. (e.g., Pilonidal sinus, TB sinus). Management: Treat underlying cause, Excision of tract (sinusectomy).
*   **Fistula:** Abnormal tract lined by granulation tissue OR epithelium, connecting two epithelial surfaces (e.g., Enterocutaneous, Vesicovaginal, Tracheo-oesophageal). Management: Treat cause (sepsis, obstruction distal, malignancy, foreign body), Nutritional support, Protect skin, Definitive surgical repair (fistulectomy, closure/resection).
*   **Malignancies:**
    *   **Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC):** MC skin Ca. Locally invasive, rarely metastasizes. Sun exposure. Pearly nodule, telangiectasia, rolled edge, central ulceration (rodent ulcer). Face (above line from angle of mouth to ear lobe). Dx: Biopsy. Tx: Excision (Mohs if critical site), Radiotherapy, Cryo, Topical (Imiquimod).
    *   **Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC):** Arises from keratinocytes. Sun exposure, chronic ulcers (Marjolin's), immunosuppression, HPV, arsenic. Scaly papule/plaque, ulcerated/keratotic nodule, everted edge. Potential for LN mets. Dx: Biopsy. Tx: Wide local excision, +/- LN dissection, Radiotherapy.
    *   **Melanoma:** Malignancy of melanocytes. UV exposure, fair skin, multiple moles, family hx. ABCDE criteria (Asymmetry, Border irregularity, Colour variation, Diameter >6mm, Evolution). Types: Superficial spreading (MC), Nodular (worst prognosis), Lentigo maligna, Acral lentiginous. Dx: Excisional biopsy (full thickness). Staging (Breslow thickness, Clark level, SLNB). Tx: Wide local excision, SLNB/LN dissection, +/- Adjuvant (Immunotherapy/Targeted).

**5 marks - Surgical Infections [as 1m]**

*   **Definition & Pathogenesis:** Infection occurring in surgical patient/related to procedure. Pathogenesis: Contamination (Endogenous - patient's flora; Exogenous - environment, staff, instruments) + Compromised host defences (diabetes, malnutrition, steroids, immunosuppression, poor perfusion) + Virulence of organism.
*   **Aetiology:** Common organisms: Staph aureus (MC SSI), Coag-neg Staph (implants), E.coli, Pseudomonas, Klebsiella (GI/GU), Streptococcus, Anaerobes (Clostridium, Bacteroides).
*   **Surgical Site Infection (SSI):** Occurs within 30 days (or 1 yr if implant). Classification: Superficial incisional, Deep incisional, Organ/Space. Features: Pain, erythema, swelling, warmth, purulent discharge, fever, leukocytosis.
*   **Antibiotics:**
    *   **Prophylactic:** Prevent infection. Given pre-op (within 60 min of incision), single dose usually. Choice based on likely contaminants for specific surgery (e.g., Cefazolin for most clean/clean-contaminated; add Metronidazole for colorectal). Not needed for clean surgery unless implant.
    *   **Therapeutic:** Treat established infection. Guided by clinical picture & culture/sensitivity results. Broader spectrum initially, then narrowed. Duration based on response.

**5 marks - Wound Healing + Care [as 3m]**

*   **Normal Wound Healing Phases:**
    1.  **Hemostasis:** Immediate. Vasoconstriction, platelet plug, coagulation cascade -> Fibrin clot.
    2.  **Inflammation:** Day 0-5. Neutrophils (early) then Macrophages arrive. Phagocytosis of debris/bacteria. Release of cytokines/growth factors. Cardinal signs present.
    3.  **Proliferation:** Day 3 - Week 3. Angiogenesis (new vessels), Fibroplasia (fibroblasts lay collagen), Epithelialization (keratinocytes migrate across), Granulation tissue formation. Wound contraction.
    4.  **Remodeling (Maturation):** Week 3 - Months/Years. Collagen reorganizes (Type III -> Type I), cross-linking increases tensile strength (max ~80% of original). Scar forms, softens, flattens.
*   **Factors Affecting Healing:**
    *   Local: Infection (most important), Poor blood supply/Ischemia, Foreign body, Hematoma, Poor surgical technique (tension), Radiation.
    *   Systemic: Age, Malnutrition (protein, Vit C, Zinc), Co-morbidities (Diabetes, CRF, Liver disease), Medications (Steroids, Chemo), Smoking, Obesity, Immunosuppression.
*   **Types of Wounds & Management:**
    *   Types: Incised, Lacerated, Abrasion, Contusion, Puncture, Avulsion, Burn, Ulcer. Clean, Contaminated, Dirty. Acute vs Chronic.
    *   Management Plan:
        *   Assessment: History, Exam, Neurovascular status.
        *   Preparation: Analgesia, Irrigation (clean), Debridement (remove foreign/devitalized tissue).
        *   Closure:
            *   Primary Closure: Immediate suture (clean wounds).
            *   Delayed Primary Closure: Leave open initially (contaminated), close after few days if clean.
            *   Secondary Intention: Leave open to heal by granulation, contraction, epithelialization (infected/large tissue loss).
        *   Dressings: Protect, absorb exudate, maintain moist environment, provide compression. Types (simple gauze, non-adherent, hydrocolloid, alginate, foam, VAC).
        *   Other: Tetanus prophylaxis, Antibiotics (if indicated), Splinting, Elevation. Observe for complications (infection, dehiscence, hematoma).
*   **Medico-legal Aspects:** Accurate documentation (wound description, mechanism, management), Informed consent for procedures, Following protocols, Reporting incidents (e.g., needlestick), Maintaining confidentiality. Clear communication with patient.

**5 marks - Shock + Management [as 5m]**

*   **Pathophysiology:** Global tissue hypoperfusion -> Inadequate O2 delivery -> Cellular hypoxia -> Switch to anaerobic metabolism -> Lactic acidosis -> Cellular dysfunction/death -> SIRS -> MODS -> Death if untreated. Compensatory mechanisms (tachycardia, vasoconstriction, RAAS activation) attempt to maintain BP & perfusion initially.
*   **Types of Shock:**
    *   **Hypovolemic:** Reduced intravascular volume. Hemorrhagic (trauma, GI bleed) vs Non-hemorrhagic (burns, dehydration, D/V, pancreatitis). **Cold & Clammy.** Low CVP, Low CO, High SVR.
    *   **Cardiogenic:** Pump failure (MI, arrhythmia, valve failure, cardiomyopathy). **Cold & Clammy.** High CVP, Low CO, High SVR. Pulmonary edema common.
    *   **Distributive:** Vasodilation & maldistribution of blood flow.
        *   Septic: Infection -> SIRS. **Warm & Flushed initially** (hyperdynamic) -> Cold (hypodynamic). Low/Normal CVP, High CO (early) / Low CO (late), Low SVR.
        *   Anaphylactic: Allergic reaction -> Mast cell degranulation (Histamine). Vasodilation, bronchospasm, urticaria. Low CVP, Low CO, Low SVR.
        *   Neurogenic: Loss of sympathetic tone (spinal cord injury >T6). Bradycardia, Hypotension. **Warm & Dry.** Low CVP, Low CO, Low SVR.
    *   **Obstructive:** Physical obstruction to filling/emptying of heart. Tension pneumothorax, Cardiac tamponade, Massive PE, Aortic stenosis. Distended JVP. High CVP, Low CO, High SVR.
*   **Principles of Resuscitation:** ABCDE Approach first!
    *   **Airway:** Secure airway (may need intubation).
    *   **Breathing:** High flow O2. Treat underlying cause (e.g., tension pneumo).
    *   **Circulation:**
        *   IV access (2 large bore cannulae).
        *   **Fluid Replacement (Hypovolemic/Distributive):** Rapid infusion of Crystalloids (e.g., 1-2L bolus Normal Saline/Hartmann's). Assess response (HR, BP, UO, Lactate). Blood products (packed RBCs, FFP, platelets) if hemorrhagic shock (aim 1:1:1 ratio).
        *   **Vasopressors (Distributive/Cardiogenic):** If fluids insufficient. Noradrenaline (1st line septic/distributive), Dobutamine (cardiogenic), Adrenaline (anaphylactic).
        *   **Address Cause:** Control bleeding, Treat infection (antibiotics, source control), Reperfuse MI, Drain tamponade/tension pneumo, Give Adrenaline/steroids/antihistamines for anaphylaxis.
*   **Monitoring:** Continuous ECG, HR, BP (invasive arterial line if needed), SpO2, RR, Temp. Urine output (catheterize - aim >0.5ml/kg/hr). CVP (guide fluid status - interpret with caution), Lactate levels (marker of perfusion), ABG (acidosis).

**4 marks - Adrenal Glands and other Endocrine Glands [as 3m]**

*   **Adrenal Anatomy:** Retroperitoneal, superior to kidneys. R gland pyramidal, L gland semilunar. Rich blood supply (Sup, Middle, Inf adrenal arteries). Single central vein (R drains to IVC, L drains to L renal vein). Cortex (Zona Glomerulosa - Aldosterone, Fasciculata - Cortisol, Reticularis - Androgens) & Medulla (Catecholamines - Adrenaline, Noradrenaline).
*   **Disorders of Adrenal Gland:**
    *   **Etiology/Features/Management:**
        *   **Cushing's Syndrome (Cortisol Excess):** Causes: Exogenous steroids (MC), Pituitary adenoma (Cushing's disease), Adrenal adenoma/carcinoma, Ectopic ACTH. Features: Central obesity, Moon facies, Buffalo hump, Striae, HTN, Hyperglycemia, Osteoporosis, Easy bruising, Mood changes. Ix: Dexamethasone suppression test, 24hr UFC, ACTH levels, Imaging (CT/MRI adrenals/pituitary). Tx: Treat cause (Surgery for tumors, Taper steroids).
        *   **Conn's Syndrome (Primary Hyperaldosteronism):** Causes: Adrenal adenoma (MC), Bilateral hyperplasia. Features: HTN, Hypokalemia, Metabolic alkalosis. Ix: Aldosterone/Renin ratio, Saline suppression test, CT Adrenals, Adrenal vein sampling. Tx: Adenoma (Adrenalectomy), Hyperplasia (Spironolactone/Eplerenone).
        *   **Addison's Disease (Primary Adrenal Insufficiency):** Causes: Autoimmune (MC), TB, Mets, Hemorrhage. Features: Weakness, Fatigue, Weight loss, Hypotension, Hyperpigmentation (ACTH excess), Hyponatremia, Hyperkalemia. Ix: Short Synacthen test, ACTH levels. Tx: Glucocorticoid (Hydrocortisone) & Mineralocorticoid (Fludrocortisone) replacement. Addisonian crisis = emergency (IV fluids, IV hydrocortisone).
        *   **Pheochromocytoma (Catecholamine Excess):** Tumor of adrenal medulla (or extra-adrenal paraganglioma). Features: Paroxysmal HTN, Headache, Palpitations, Sweating (Triad). Rule of 10s (10% bilateral, 10% extra-adrenal, 10% malignant, 10% familial). Ix: 24hr Urine/Plasma metanephrines, CT/MRI, MIBG scan. Tx: Pre-op alpha-blockade (Phenoxybenzamine) then beta-blockade. Surgical resection (Adrenalectomy).
*   **Adrenal Tumors (Incidentaloma):** Often found on imaging for other reasons. Assess: Functioning? Malignant? Size (>4-6cm more likely malignant). Ix: Hormonal workup (as above), CT features (size, density, margins). Tx: Resect if functioning, malignant suspicion, or large (>4-6cm). Observe if small, non-functioning, benign features.

**4 marks - Transplantation [as 3m] [as 3m]**

*   **Immunological Basis:** Rejection is immune response to foreign antigens (Major Histocompatibility Complex - MHC, aka Human Leukocyte Antigen - HLA). T-cells (Cellular rejection - direct/indirect recognition) & B-cells (Antibody-mediated/Humoral rejection - preformed/de novo antibodies against HLA/ABO). Goal of immunosuppression is to prevent this.
*   **Principles of Immunosuppressive Therapy:** Balance between preventing rejection and avoiding side effects (infection, malignancy). Usually multi-drug approach targeting different pathways.
    *   Induction: Intense therapy at time of transplant (e.g., Anti-IL2R Ab - Basiliximab, Depleting Ab - ATG).
    *   Maintenance: Long-term therapy. Triple therapy common:
        1.  Calcineurin Inhibitor (CNI): Tacrolimus or Ciclosporin (Inhibit IL-2 production). Nephrotoxic.
        2.  Antiproliferative: Mycophenolate Mofetil (MMF) or Azathioprine (Inhibit lymphocyte proliferation). GI upset, BM suppression.
        3.  Steroids: Prednisolone (Anti-inflammatory, suppress cytokines). Multiple side effects. Steroid avoidance/withdrawal protocols used.
    *   Rejection Treatment: High dose steroids (pulse methylpred), Depleting antibodies (ATG) for severe/steroid-resistant rejection.
*   **Organ Transplantation (Indications, Principles, Management):**
    *   **Indications:** End-stage organ failure (Kidney - ESRD, Liver - Cirrhosis/ALF/HCC, Heart - Cardiomyopathy, Lung - CF/COPD, Pancreas - T1DM).
    *   **Surgical Principles:** Donor selection (living/deceased), Recipient workup & matching (ABO, HLA), Organ retrieval & preservation (cold ischemia time), Implantation surgery (vascular/ductal/airway anastomoses), Peri-operative care.
    *   **Management:** Lifelong immunosuppression, Monitoring for rejection (biopsy, function tests), Monitoring/managing complications (infection - CMV/BK virus, malignancy - PTLD/skin Ca, drug toxicity, CVD, diabetes).
*   **Legal & Ethical Issues:** Brainstem death criteria, Consent (Opt-in vs Opt-out systems), Donor allocation fairness, Living donation (coercion, risk to donor), Commercialization/Transplant tourism, Confidentiality.

**4 marks - Salivary Glands + Neck Cysts + Cervical Lymphadenitis [as 5m] [as 1m]**

*   **Salivary Gland Disorders:**
    *   **Anatomy:** Parotid (Stensen's duct), Submandibular (Wharton's duct), Sublingual. Facial nerve runs through parotid. Lingual & Hypoglossal nerves related to submandibular duct.
    *   **Pathology/Clinical Presentation:**
        *   Sialadenitis: Inflammation. Acute (bacterial/viral-mumps) - painful swelling, +/- pus. Chronic - recurrent swelling, +/- stones.
        *   Sialolithiasis (Stones): MC in Submandibular (viscous, alkaline saliva; duct runs uphill). Colicky pain/swelling on eating. Stone may be palpable.
        *   Tumors: Benign (Pleomorphic adenoma - MC, Warthin's tumor). Malignant (Mucoepidermoid Ca - MC overall malignant, Adenoid cystic Ca - perineural invasion common). Present as painless mass (MC), Facial nerve palsy suggests malignancy in parotid.
    *   **Investigations:** USG (stones, cystic/solid), CT/MRI (extent, deep lobe, LNs), Sialogram (duct structure - less used), FNAC (tumor diagnosis - good for parotid), Core biopsy (less common).
    *   **Principles of Treatment:** Sialadenitis (Antibiotics, hydration, sialogogues), Stones (Conservative, surgical removal - intraoral/gland excision), Tumors: Parotidectomy (Superficial or Total, with Facial nerve preservation), Submandibular gland excision. +/- Neck dissection/Radiotherapy for malignancy.
*   **Neck Cysts:**
    *   **Thyroglossal Cyst:** Midline, moves on swallowing/tongue protrusion. Remnant of thyroglossal duct. Tx: Sistrunk operation (excise cyst + tract + central hyoid bone).
    *   **Branchial Cyst:** Lateral neck, anterior border SCM. Remnant of branchial cleft (usually 2nd). Smooth, fluctuant. Tx: Excision.
    *   **Cystic Hygroma:** Lymphatic malformation. Soft, cystic, transilluminates brilliantly. Usually posterior triangle, axilla. Tx: Sclerotherapy, Excision.
    *   **Dermoid Cyst:** Midline (submental/suprasternal) or Lateral. Doughy feel. Tx: Excision.
*   **Cervical Lymphadenitis:** Enlarged lymph nodes.
    *   Causes: Reactive (infection - viral/bacterial), Infective (TB - MC chronic, Cat scratch, Toxoplasmosis), Malignant (Lymphoma, Metastatic - SCC from head/neck, thyroid, lung).
    *   Ix: FBC, ESR, Serology, CXR, US neck, FNAC/Core biopsy/Excision biopsy (Gold standard for lymphoma).
    *   Tx: Treat underlying cause (Antibiotics, Anti-TB, Chemo/Radio for malignancy).

**4 marks - Burns [as 3m]**

*   **History & Examination:** Mechanism (flame, scald, chemical, electrical), Time, Environment (closed space - inhalation?), Substances involved, First aid given. Primary Survey (ABCDE), Secondary Survey (Full exam).
    *   **Assess Burn:** Site, Size (% TBSA - Rule of Nines, Lund-Browder chart, Palmar surface ~1%), Depth (Superficial/1st°, Partial thickness - superficial or deep/2°, Full thickness/3°, 4th°). Signs of inhalation injury (soot, singed hairs, hoarseness, stridor). Circumferential burns (risk of compartment syndrome).
*   **Pathophysiology:** Heat -> Cell damage/necrosis. Inflammatory mediators -> Increased capillary permeability -> Fluid shift (intravascular to interstitial) -> Edema, Hypovolemia (Burn shock). Immunosuppression. Hypermetabolism.
*   **Clinical Features, Diagnosis, Treatment:**
    *   **Features:** Pain (partial thickness), No pain (full thickness), Blisters, Eschar (full thickness), Edema. Systemic: Hypovolemia, Shock, +/- Inhalation injury signs.
    *   **Diagnosis:** Based on history and clinical assessment of depth/TBSA.
    *   **Treatment:**
        1.  **Stop Burning Process:** Remove source, Cool with running water (10-20 min).
        2.  **ABCDE & Resuscitation:** High flow O2. Secure airway early if inhalation suspected. IV access (large bore, through unburnt skin if possible). Fluid Resuscitation (Parkland Formula: 4ml x %TBSA x Wt(kg) - give half over 1st 8hrs from time of burn, rest over next 16hrs). Use Crystalloids (Hartmann's/RL). Monitor Urine Output (aim 0.5-1ml/kg/hr adults, higher in children/electrical).
        3.  **Analgesia:** IV opioids (Morphine).
        4.  **Wound Care:** Clean, Debride blisters(?), Apply dressings (Silver sulfadiazine, non-adherent gauze, specialized dressings). Escharotomy if circumferential burn compromising limb/chest wall.
        5.  **Other:** Tetanus prophylaxis, NG tube (if >20% TBSA - ileus risk), Catheter, Nutritional support (high protein/calorie), +/- Antibiotics (topical, systemic if septic). Infection control. Early excision & grafting for deep partial/full thickness burns. Rehabilitation.
*   **Medicolegal Aspects:** Accurate documentation, Photographs, Reporting (if non-accidental injury suspected), Consent for procedures.

**4 marks - Blood and Blood Components [as 5m] [as 1m]**

*   **Indications & Appropriate Use:**
    *   **Packed Red Blood Cells (PRBCs):** Increase O2 carrying capacity. Symptomatic anemia (Hb <7-8 g/dL, or higher if cardiac disease/ongoing bleeding), Acute blood loss (>15-20% volume).
    *   **Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP):** Replace coagulation factors. Massive transfusion, DIC, Liver disease coagulopathy, Warfarin reversal (urgent), Inherited factor deficiencies (if specific concentrate unavailable). Requires ABO compatibility.
    *   **Platelets:** Treat/prevent bleeding due to thrombocytopenia or platelet dysfunction. Prophylactic (Plt <10k, or <20k if septic/fever), Therapeutic (Plt <50k + active bleeding/surgery).
    *   **Cryoprecipitate:** Contains Fibrinogen, Factor VIII, Factor XIII, vWF. Used for Hypofibrinogenemia (e.g., DIC, massive transfusion), specific factor deficiencies.
    *   **Albumin:** Volume expansion (controversial), specific conditions (paracentesis, SBP).
*   **Complications of Blood Transfusion:**
    *   **Immunological:**
        *   Acute Hemolytic Transfusion Reaction (AHTR): ABO incompatibility (clerical error!). Fever, chills, pain, hemoglobinuria, DIC, shock, renal failure. STOP transfusion immediately, resuscitate.
        *   Febrile Non-Hemolytic Reaction (FNHTR): MC reaction. Cytokines from donor WBCs. Fever, chills. Slow/stop transfusion, antipyretics. Use Leuko-depleted products.
        *   Allergic Reaction: Urticaria, flushing, pruritus. Antihistamines.
        *   Anaphylactic Reaction: Severe allergy (often IgA deficiency). Hypotension, bronchospasm. STOP transfusion, Adrenaline, steroids.
        *   Transfusion-Related Acute Lung Injury (TRALI): Donor antibodies vs recipient WBCs -> Pulmonary edema. Respiratory distress within 6 hrs. Supportive care (O2, ventilation).
        *   Transfusion-Associated Graft vs Host Disease (TA-GVHD): Donor lymphocytes attack recipient. Rare, often fatal. Irradiation of products for high-risk patients.
        *   Post-Transfusion Purpura (PTP): Delayed thrombocytopenia.
    *   **Infectious:** HBV, HCV, HIV, Syphilis, Malaria, CMV, Bacterial contamination (esp platelets). Rigorous screening minimizes risk.
    *   **Non-Immunological:**
        *   Volume Overload (TACO): Esp in elderly/cardiac patients. Dyspnea, HTN, raised JVP. Slow transfusion, diuretics.
        *   Iron Overload: Chronic transfusions. Requires chelation therapy.
        *   Hypothermia: Rapid infusion of cold blood. Use blood warmer.
        *   Electrolyte Imbalance: Hyperkalemia (older blood), Hypocalcemia (citrate toxicity in massive transfusion).

**3 marks - Oropharyngeal Carcinoma**

*   **Etiopathogenesis:** MC type is SCC (~90%). Risk factors: Tobacco (smoking, chewing), Alcohol (synergistic), HPV (esp HPV-16, increasing incidence, better prognosis), Betel nut chewing, Poor oral hygiene, Chronic irritation, Immunosuppression, Genetic factors. Precursor lesions: Leukoplakia, Erythroplakia.
*   **Symptoms & Signs:** Non-healing ulcer, Pain (may radiate to ear - otalgia), Lump/swelling, Dysphagia/Odynophagia, Trismus (pterygoid invasion), Loose teeth, Ill-fitting dentures, Neck lump (LN mets). Speech changes (hot potato voice). Halitosis.

**3 marks - Basic Surgical Skills**

*   **Aseptic Techniques, Sterilization, Disinfection:**
    *   **Asepsis:** Practices preventing microbial contamination. Hand washing/scrubbing, Sterile gloves/gown/drapes, Theatre discipline, Skin prep (antiseptic).
    *   **Sterilization:** Complete elimination of all microorganisms including spores. Methods: Autoclave (Steam under pressure - MC for instruments), Dry Heat (oven), Ethylene Oxide (gas - heat sensitive items), Radiation (gamma), Chemical (Glutaraldehyde - endoscopes, cold sterilization).
    *   **Disinfection:** Reduction of harmful microorganisms (not spores). Methods: Chemical disinfectants (Alcohol, Chlorhexidine, Povidone-iodine - skin prep; Hypochlorite - surfaces). Pasteurization (heat).
*   **Surgical Approaches, Incisions, Instruments:**
    *   **Approaches:** Based on target organ, exposure needed, cosmesis, previous surgery. Open vs MIS.
    *   **Incisions:** Follow Langer's lines where possible. Examples: Midline laparotomy, Kocher's (subcostal), McBurney's (appendicectomy), Pfannenstiel (pelvic), Transverse. Layers incised sequentially.
    *   **Instruments:** Basic set: Scalpel, Forceps (toothed/non-toothed, dissecting/tissue), Scissors (Mayo/Metzenbaum), Hemostats (artery forceps - Spencer Wells, Mosquito), Needle holder, Retractors (self-retaining/handheld), Suction. Specialized instruments for specific procedures. Proper handling & care essential.
*   **Wound Closure & Anastomosis Materials/Methods:**
    *   **Wound Closure:** Layered closure (peritoneum, fascia, fat, skin). Methods: Sutures, Staples, Clips, Tissue adhesives (glue), Steri-strips.
    *   **Sutures:**
        *   Materials: Absorbable (Vicryl, Monocryl, PDS, Catgut - natural) vs Non-absorbable (Prolene, Nylon, Silk - natural, Steel). Monofilament vs Multifilament (braided - higher infection risk).
        *   Needles: Cutting vs Tapered, Straight vs Curved.
        *   Techniques: Interrupted, Continuous, Subcuticular, Mattress.
    *   **Knots:** Secure knots essential (Reef knot). Proper technique to avoid slipping.
    *   **Anastomosis:** Joining hollow viscera (bowel, vessel). Techniques: Hand-sewn (single/double layer, interrupted/continuous) or Stapled (Linear/Circular staplers - GIA, EEA). Principles: Good blood supply, No tension, Accurate apposition, Watertight/Airtight seal.

**2 marks - Biohazard Disposal**

*   **Classification:** Based on risk/type. Common categories (may vary slightly):
    *   General Waste (Black bag): Non-infectious office/kitchen waste.
    *   Infectious Waste (Yellow bag/container): Contaminated with blood/body fluids (dressings, swabs, gloves, cultures). Requires incineration or autoclaving before disposal.
    *   Sharps Waste (Yellow puncture-proof container): Needles, scalpels, blades, broken glass. Requires incineration or encapsulation.
    *   Cytotoxic Waste (Purple bag/container): Chemotherapy drugs/items contaminated. Requires specific high-temp incineration.
    *   Radioactive Waste: Requires specific handling/decay storage according to regulations.
    *   Anatomical Waste (Red bag): Body parts, placentas. Requires incineration.
*   **Methods of Disposal:** Segregation at source is crucial. Appropriate color-coded bags/containers. Secure handling & transport. Treatment: Incineration (high temp, destroys pathogens & reduces volume), Autoclaving (sterilizes infectious waste), Chemical disinfection, Secure landfill (for treated/general waste). Follow local/national guidelines strictly. Training of staff is essential.

**2 marks - Congenital Facial Anomalies [as 1m]**

*   **Cleft Lip & Palate:**
    *   **Etiology:** Multifactorial. Failure of fusion of facial processes during embryonic development (Lip ~4-7 wks, Palate ~7-12 wks). Genetic predisposition + Environmental factors (smoking, alcohol, certain meds - phenytoin, folate deficiency?). Associated syndromes (Pierre Robin).
    *   **Classification:** Based on structures involved. Lip (Unilateral/Bilateral, Complete/Incomplete), Palate (Soft/Hard/Both, Submucous), Alveolus. Can occur in isolation or combination (CLP = Cleft Lip & Palate).
    *   **Principles of Reconstruction:** MDT approach (Plastic surgeon, Paediatrician, Orthodontist, ENT, Speech therapist). Timing:
        *   Lip Repair: Rule of 10s (10 weeks old, 10 pounds weight, Hb 10 g/dL). Millard rotation-advancement flap (unilateral), Mulliken repair (bilateral).
        *   Palate Repair: Around 9-12 months (before significant speech development). Various techniques (e.g., Von Langenbeck, Wardill-Kilner V-Y pushback, Furlow double opposing Z-plasty). Goal is watertight closure & functional soft palate.
        *   Alveolar Bone Grafting: Around 8-10 years (before canine eruption).
        *   Secondary procedures: Rhinoplasty, Pharyngoplasty (for VPI), Orthognathic surgery. Long term follow-up needed.

**2 marks - Surgical Audit + Research + Ethics [as 3m]**

    *   **Planning & Conduct:** Audit Cycle: 1. Identify problem/Set standard. 2. Collect data. 3. Analyse data (compare performance vs standard). 4. Implement change. 5. Re-audit. *   **Clinical Research Principles & Steps:** Systematic investigation to generate new knowledge.
    *   Principles: Ethical conduct (Helsinki), Scientific validity, Informed consent, Equipoise (RCTs), Minimize bias.
    *   Steps: 1. Formulate research question. 2. Literature review. 3. Design study (Observational - cohort/case-control; Experimental - RCT). 4. Obtain ethical approval. 5. Collect data. 6. Analyse data. 7. Interpret results & Disseminate findings (publication/presentation).

    *   **Non-maleficence:** Do no harm (primum non nocere).

**2 marks - Metabolic Response to Injury [as 1m]**

*   **Basic Concepts of Homeostasis:** Maintaining stable internal environment (temp, pH, fluid balance, glucose etc.) despite external changes. Involves sensors, control center, effectors (negative feedback loops). Injury disrupts homeostasis.
*   **Metabolic Changes & Mediators:** Biphasic response:
    *   **Ebb Phase (0-48 hrs):** Decreased metabolic rate, Temp, O2 consumption. Hypovolemia. Aim = conserve energy. Mediated by initial neuro-hormonal surge.
    *   **Flow Phase (Days-Weeks):** Hypermetabolism, Catabolism. Increased metabolic rate, O2 consumption, Temp. Breakdown of protein (muscle) & fat for energy/substrates. Insulin resistance -> Hyperglycemia. Na+/Water retention.
    *   **Mediators:** Neuroendocrine (Catecholamines, Cortisol, Glucagon, Growth Hormone, ADH, Aldosterone) & Inflammatory (Cytokines - TNF-a, IL-1, IL-6; Prostaglandins, Complement).
*   **Factors Affecting Response:** Severity of injury, Presence of sepsis, Nutritional status, Age, Co-morbidities, Temperature, Pain, Anesthesia/Surgery.
*   **Basic Concepts of Perioperative Care related to Response:** Minimize surgical stress (MIS), Maintain normothermia, Adequate analgesia, Early mobilization, Early nutritional support (Enteral preferred), Glycemic control, Optimize oxygen delivery. Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS) protocols aim to attenuate this response.
