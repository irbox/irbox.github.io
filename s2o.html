Abdomen (Including Hernias)

(Mark Allocation: 12 Marks)

Q: Define hernia. Classify inguinal hernias.

Definition: Abnormal protrusion of a viscus or a part of it through a weak point in the body (opening).

Classification (Inguinal):

  • Anatomical:
    1. Indirect hernia (through deep ring)
    2. Direct hernia (through Hesselbach's triangle)
  • Nyhus Classification (Based on defect):
    • Type 1: Indirect, normal deep ring.
    • Type 2: Indirect, dilated deep ring.
    • Type 3: Posterior wall defect (a: Direct, b: Pantaloon/Sliding, c: Femoral).
    • Type 4: Recurrent hernia.
  • European Hernia Society: Primary(P)/Recurrent(R); Lateral(L)/Medial(M)/Femoral(F); Defect size (cm). E.g., PM2.
  • Types of Indirect Hernia: Complete (Scrotal), Funicular, Bubonocoele.
Q: Describe the boundaries and contents of the inguinal canal.

Length: 4 cm, deep to superficial ring.

Boundaries (MALT - 2 layers deep from Ant->Sup->Inf->Post):

  • Anterior: Ext. oblique aponeurosis; laterally Int. oblique.
  • Posterior: Fascia transversalis; medially Conjoined tendon.
  • Superior (Roof): Arched fibres of Int. oblique & Transversus abdominis.
  • Inferior (Floor): Inguinal ligament; medially Lacunar ligament.

Contents:

  1. Spermatic cord (M) / Round ligament (F)
  2. Ilioinguinal nerve
  3. Genital branch of genitofemoral nerve
  4. Vestigial processus vaginalis
Q: Describe the Lichtenstein repair for inguinal hernia.

Principle: Tension-free repair reinforcing posterior wall with synthetic (Polypropylene) mesh.

Procedure Outline:

  • Inguinal incision, dissect sac.
  • Herniotomy (indirect) or inversion (direct) of sac.
  • Place mesh (approx. 8x16 cm) over posterior wall.
  • Fixation: Inferiorly to inguinal/lacunar lig.; Medially overlaps pubic tubercle (to rectus sheath); Laterally slit for cord, tails sutured beyond deep ring; Superiorly to conjoined tendon/int. oblique.
  • Creates artificial deep ring.
  • Close external oblique over cord, then skin.
Q: What are the complications of inguinal hernia surgery?
  • Intra-op: Vessel injury (iliac), Bladder injury, Vas/Testicular artery injury.
  • Early Post-op: Pain, Bleeding/Haematoma, Urinary retention, Scrotal oedema.
  • Intermediate: Seroma, Surgical site infection (SSI), Mesh infection.
  • Late: Inguinodynia (chronic pain >3m - nerve injury), Ischaemic orchitis/atrophy, Recurrence.
Q: Causes and management of intestinal obstruction? (SE/LE)

Definition: Failure of intestinal contents to move distally.

Causes:

  • Small Bowel (Common): Adhesions (most common post-surgery), Hernias (obstructed), Volvulus, Strictures (TB, Crohn's), Gallstone ileus.
  • Large Bowel: Carcinoma (most common), Volvulus (sigmoid), Diverticular stricture, Faecal impaction.

Clinical Features (Cardinal Signs - PVDC):

  1. Pain (Colicky)
  2. Vomiting (Early/profuse in high, Late/faeculent in low)
  3. Distension (Central in SB, Peripheral in LB)
  4. Constipation (Absolute)

Strangulation signs: Constant severe pain, shock, tenderness, rigidity, fever.

Investigations: Blood tests (CBC, U&E), X-ray Abdomen Erect & Supine (Air-fluid levels, dilated loops), CT Scan (Gold standard - site, cause, complications).

Management Principles (ABCDEF / Drip & Suck):

  • Aspiration: Ryle's tube (NG decompression).
  • Bowel rest: Nil by mouth (NPO).
  • Charts: Vitals, Input/Output.
  • Drugs: IV Antibiotics, Analgesia.
  • Electrolytes/Fluids: IV fluid resuscitation (Crystalloids).
  • F Exploratory Laparotomy: If strangulation, perforation, or failed conservative Mx. Identify cause, assess viability, relieve obstruction, resect non-viable bowel (+/- stoma).
  • Conservative Trial: For adhesions, incomplete obstruction (monitor 48-72h).
Q: Discuss Appendicitis (Etiology, CF, Management, Complications) (SE)

Definition: Inflammation of appendix.

Etiology: Obstruction (Faecolith, lymphoid hyperplasia) or non-obstructive infection.

Clinical Features:

  • Symptoms: Migratory Pain (Periumbilical -> RIF), Anorexia (key), Nausea/Vomiting, Low fever.
  • Signs: RIF tenderness (McBurney's point), Rebound/Guarding, Rovsing's, Psoas, Obturator signs.

Investigations: Usually clinical. Leucocytosis. Imaging (USG/CT) if uncertain (esp. CT). Alvarado score.

Management: Appendicectomy (Laparoscopic preferred).

  • Pre-op: NPO, IV Fluids, IV Antibiotics.
  • Surgery: Identify base (trace taeniae), ligate mesoappendix/artery, ligate & divide base.

Complications: Perforation, Mass, Abscess, Wound infection, Obstruction (late).

Q: Management of Appendicular Mass. (SA/SE)

Definition: Inflammatory phlegmon in RIF (>48-72h onset).

Management (Ochsner-Sherren Regime - Conservative):

  • Admit, Bed rest (Fowler's position).
  • NPO, IV Fluids.
  • Charts (TPR, BP, Mass size).
  • IV Antibiotics (Broad spectrum).
  • Analgesia. NO purgatives.

Monitor for Improvement: Decreased pain/size, settling vitals, return of appetite.

Monitor for Deterioration (Needs Intervention): Rising pulse (key!), increasing pain/size, persistent vomiting/fever, peritonism.

Intervention if Deteriorating: USG/CT for abscess -> Percutaneous/Surgical drainage. Laparotomy if peritonitis.

Interval Appendicectomy: Controversial (6-8 weeks later). Consider if recurrent symptoms or suspicion of underlying pathology (esp. >40 yrs).

Q: Peritonitis Principles. (SE)

Definition: Inflammation of peritoneum.

Classification: Primary (SBP), Secondary (Source within abdomen - commonest), Tertiary (Persistent). Localized vs Generalized.

Clinical Features (Generalized): Severe pain, distension, vomiting, fever, shock signs. Abdomen: Tenderness, Guarding, Rigidity, Rebound, Absent bowel sounds.

Management Principles (Resuscitate -> Source Control -> Antibiotics):

  1. Resuscitation: ABCDE, IV fluids (aggressive), NGT, Catheter, Analgesia.
  2. Antibiotics: Prompt IV broad-spectrum (Gram +ve/-ve, anaerobes).
  3. Source Control (Surgery - Laparotomy): Identify & treat cause (e.g., repair perforation, resect gangrene), thorough peritoneal lavage, drainage. Decision re: anastomosis vs stoma.
Q: Femoral Hernia. (SE)

Definition: Herniation through femoral canal (medial to vein, below inguinal ligament).

Anatomy: Rigid femoral ring boundaries (Inguinal lig., Lacunar lig., Pectineal lig., Femoral vein).

CF: Swelling below & lateral to pubic tubercle. Often irreducible/strangulates easily (high risk!). Cough impulse often absent. Commoner in women.

Management: Always surgical repair (high strangulation risk). Low (Lockwood), High (McEvedy), or Inguinal approach. Close ring (Inguinal lig to Pectineal lig). Urgent surgery if strangulated.

Q: Sigmoid Volvulus. (SE)

Definition: Twisting of sigmoid colon on its mesentery.

Etiology: Long sigmoid/mesentery, narrow base, chronic constipation, elderly.

CF: Acute onset severe pain, massive distension (tympanitic), absolute constipation. Vomiting late.

Ix: X-ray Abdomen ('Coffee bean' sign). Contrast enema ('Bird's beak'). CT confirms.

Mx: Resuscitate. Non-operative decompression (Sigmoidoscopy + rectal tube) if no gangrene/perforation. Surgery if failed decompression, signs of complications, or electively after decompression (Resection +/- anastomosis or Hartmann's) to prevent recurrence.

Q: Intussusception. (SE)

Definition: Telescoping of bowel segment into adjacent distal segment.

Types: Ileocolic (most common), Ileo-ileal etc. Idiopathic (infants, post-viral) vs Pathological lead point (older children/adults - Meckel's, polyp, tumour).

CF (Infant Triad): 1. Intermittent colicky pain (screaming, drawing legs up). 2. Palpable sausage-shaped mass (RUQ/epigastrium). 3. "Red currant jelly" stool (late).

Ix: Ultrasound (Investigation of choice - 'Target'/'Pseudokidney' sign). Air/Contrast enema (diagnostic & therapeutic in infants).

Mx: Resuscitate. Non-operative reduction (Air/hydrostatic enema) if idiopathic, stable infant. Surgery if failed reduction, peritonitis, lead point suspected, or in adults (Laparotomy/Laparoscopy -> Manual reduction or Resection).

Q: Rectal Prolapse. (SE)

Definition: Protrusion of rectum through anus.

Types: Partial (Mucosa only - radial folds) vs Complete (Full thickness/Procidentia - concentric folds).

CF: Lump protruding (esp. on straining), mucus discharge, bleeding, faecal incontinence (complete), constipation.

Mx: Conservative (children - treat constipation). Surgery (complete/symptomatic): Perineal (Delorme's, Altemeier's - higher recurrence, lower risk) vs Abdominal (Rectopexy +/- Resection - lower recurrence, higher risk).

Q: Haemorrhoids. (SA)

Definition: Symptomatic enlargement/displacement of anal cushions.

Classification: Internal (Grades I-IV based on prolapse) vs External.

CF: Painless bright red bleeding (most common), prolapse, discharge, pruritus. Pain if thrombosed/strangulated.

Mx: Conservative (fibre, fluids, avoid straining). Office procedures (Grade I-III: Rubber band ligation, Sclerotherapy). Surgery (Grade III/IV, failed conservative: Excisional haemorrhoidectomy, Stapled haemorrhoidopexy, HALO).

Q: Anal Fissure. (SA)

Definition: Tear in distal anal canal lining (usually posterior midline).

Etiology: Trauma (hard stool) -> pain -> sphincter spasm -> ischaemia -> poor healing cycle.

CF: Severe pain during/after defecation, bright red bleeding (scant). Chronic: Sentinel pile, hypertrophied papilla.

Mx: Conservative (fibre, stool softeners, Sitz baths). Medical (Topical GTN/Diltiazem - relax sphincter). Botulinum toxin. Surgery (Lateral Internal Sphincterotomy - LIS) for refractory chronic fissures.

Vascular System

(Mark Allocation: 10 Marks)

Q: Arterial occlusive disease (Atherosclerosis/TAO). (LE)

Definition: Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD) due to Atherosclerosis (common, older, risk factors - smoking, DM, HTN, lipids) or Thromboangiitis Obliterans (TAO/Buerger's - smokers, <45yrs, distal small/medium vessels + veins).

Clinical Features (Chronic Lower Limb Ischaemia):

  • Intermittent Claudication: Muscle pain on exertion, relieved by rest (calf = fem-pop; thigh/buttock = aortoiliac).
  • Rest Pain: Severe foot pain at rest/night, relieved by dependency (CLI).
  • Tissue Loss: Ulcers, gangrene (CLI = Rest pain +/- Tissue loss).
  • Signs: Absent pulses, coldness, pallor, hair loss, shiny skin, dependent rubor.

Investigations: ABPI (<0.9 claudication, <0.5 rest pain), Doppler, Duplex USG (primary Ix), CTA/MRA (planning), DSA (intervention).

Management:

  • Conservative/Medical: Risk factor modification (Smoking cessation!, Statins, BP/DM control), Exercise program, Antiplatelets (Aspirin/Clopidogrel), Cilostazol. Foot care.
  • Intervention (CLI / Disabling Claudication): Endovascular (PTA +/- Stent) for short/focal lesions. Open Surgery (Bypass grafting - vein/PTFE; Endarterectomy) for extensive disease. Amputation for non-salvageable limb.
Q: Varicocele & Management. (SA)

Definition: Dilatation of pampiniform plexus veins.

Etiology: Primary (idiopathic, left side common - anatomy) vs Secondary (obstruction - e.g., renal tumour - check if right-sided/acute onset/older patient).

CF: Usually asymptomatic. Scrotal ache/heaviness, infertility. 'Bag of worms' feel on palpation (standing, Valsalva).

Ix: Clinical. Doppler USG confirms, checks reflux, rules out secondary cause.

Management: Conservative (reassurance, support). Intervention if symptomatic, testicular atrophy, or infertility with abnormal semen: Surgical ligation (Microscopic subinguinal preferred) or Embolization.

Q: Acute Limb Ischaemia. (SE)

Definition: Sudden decrease in limb perfusion threatening viability (<2 weeks).

Causes: Embolism (Heart - AF/MI; Aneurysm) vs Thrombosis (in situ on plaque). Trauma.

CF (6 P's): Pain, Pallor, Pulselessness, Paraesthesia, Paralysis, Perishing Cold.

Mx: EMERGENCY! Heparin (IV bolus + infusion). Analgesia. Revascularization ASAP (<6h): Surgical Embolectomy (Fogarty), Catheter-directed Thrombolysis, Percutaneous Thrombectomy, or Bypass. Monitor for Reperfusion syndrome / Compartment syndrome (-> Fasciotomy). Amputation if irreversible.

Urinary System

(Mark Allocation: 10 Marks)

Q: Haematuria (Causes, CF, Ix, Mx). (LE)

Definition: Blood in urine (>3 RBCs/hpf). Macro/Microscopic.

Causes ("TSI" + others):

  • Tumour: RCC, TCC (Bladder - most common >50yr, Ureter, Pelvis), Wilms'.
  • Stone: Renal, Ureteric, Bladder.
  • Infection/Inflammation: UTI/Cystitis/Pyelonephritis, TB, Glomerulonephritis.
  • Other: BPH, Trauma, Polycystic kidneys, Structural (PUJO), Drugs (anticoagulants), Systemic.

Clinical Features: Painless (Malignancy?) vs Painful (Stone/Infection?). Timing (Initial/Terminal/Total). Clots? Associated LUTS, flank pain, systemic symptoms. Smoking Hx.

Investigations: Urine (Dipstick, Microscopy, Culture, Cytology), Bloods (FBC, U&E, PSA), Imaging (USG KUB initial -> CT Urogram = Ix of choice), Cystoscopy (essential to visualize bladder).

Management: Treat the underlying cause.

Q: Hydronephrosis (Causes, CF, Mx). (SE)

Definition: Aseptic dilatation of renal pelvis/calyces due to urine outflow obstruction.

Causes: Congenital (PUJ Obstruction - most common, VUR, PUV) vs Acquired (Stones, Strictures, Tumours - TCC/Cervix/Prostate, BPH, Retroperitoneal fibrosis).

CF: Often asymptomatic. Loin pain (dull ache, or colicky - Dietl's crisis in PUJO). Palpable mass. UTI. Haematuria. Renal failure (if bilateral/solitary).

Ix: Urine analysis, RFTs. USG KUB (confirms dilatation). CT Urogram (cause/anatomy). Isotope Renography (MAG3/DTPA - differential function & drainage, essential for PUJO).

Management: Treat cause. Relieve obstruction. PUJO: Pyeloplasty (Anderson-Hynes). Temporary drainage if sepsis/AKI (PCN/Stent). Nephrectomy if non-functioning.

Q: Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH). (Focus for Pass)

Focus:

  • Key Symptoms (LUTS): Frequency, Urgency, Nocturia (Irritative); Poor stream, Intermittency, Straining, Incomplete emptying (Obstructive). Remember FUN PISS or similar mnemonic.
  • DRE findings: Smooth, firm, symmetrically enlarged prostate, median sulcus may be obliterated.
  • Complication: Acute urinary retention. Others: Chronic retention, UTIs, stones, renal impairment.
  • Basic Mx options: Medical (Alpha-blockers, 5-alpha-reductase inhibitors) vs Surgical (TURP - Transurethral Resection of Prostate principle).

Why: Very common condition, high marks in Urinary, frequent SE/SA.

Q: Renal/Ureteric Stones. (Focus for Pass)

Focus:

  • Clinical Features: Severe, colicky loin-to-groin pain, haematuria (often microscopic), nausea/vomiting.
  • Investigation of choice: Non-Contrast CT KUB (NCCT KUB).
  • Basic Mx: Analgesia (NSAIDs often effective), Hydration, Medical Expulsive Therapy (MET - e.g., Tamsulosin) for small distal ureteric stones (<5-7mm). Intervention (ESWL, Ureteroscopy, PCNL) for larger/obstructing stones or failed MET.

Why: Very common cause of abdominal pain/haematuria.

Q: Urinary Tract Infection (UTI). (SA)

Definition: Inflammation of urothelium due to bacteria (+bacteriuria/pyuria).

Classification: Upper (Pyelonephritis) vs Lower (Cystitis). Uncomplicated vs Complicated.

Causes: E. coli (most common). Ascending route usual. Risk factors: Female, sex, obstruction, catheter, DM.

CF: Cystitis (Dysuria, Frequency, Urgency, Suprapubic pain). Pyelonephritis (Cystitis symptoms + Flank pain, Fever, Chills, Vomiting).

Ix: Urinalysis (Leucocytes, Nitrites), Microscopy (WBC, Bacteria), Culture & Sensitivity (essential in complicated/pyelo). Imaging if complicated/recurrent/pyelo not improving (USG/CT).

Mx: Antibiotics (short course oral for uncomplicated cystitis - Nitrofurantoin/TMP-SMX; longer course/IV for complicated/pyelo - Fluoroquinolones/Cephalosporins). Hydration. Treat underlying cause if any.

Q: Urethral Stricture. (SA)

Definition: Narrowing of urethra due to spongiofibrosis.

Causes: Iatrogenic (Instrumentation/Catheter - most common now), Trauma (Straddle, Pelvic #), Infection (Gonorrhea - historically), BXO.

CF: Obstructive voiding symptoms (poor stream, hesitancy, straining, incomplete emptying, dribbling), Recurrent UTIs, Retention.

Ix: Uroflowmetry (reduced peak flow). PVR (increased). RGU + VCUG (Gold standard - location/length). Urethroscopy.

Mx: Dilatation/VIU (temporary/short strictures). Urethroplasty (Open reconstruction - EPA or Substitution with graft/flap) for definitive cure (long/complex/recurrent).

Q: Posterior Urethral Valves (PUV). (SA)

Definition: Congenital obstructive membranes in male posterior urethra (most common cause of obstruction in male infants).

Pathophysiology: Obstruction -> high bladder pressure -> Bladder changes (thickening, trabeculation), Hydroureteronephrosis, VUR, Renal dysplasia/failure. Oligohydramnios -> Pulmonary hypoplasia.

CF: Antenatal USG findings. Neonate: Poor stream, straining, palpable bladder, UTI, FTT, sepsis. Child: UTI, voiding dysfunction.

Ix: USG KUB (Hydronephrosis, bladder changes). VCUG (Gold standard - dilated posterior urethra, abrupt narrowing). RFTs.

Mx: Stabilize. Drain urine (Catheter/Suprapubic). Endoscopic valve ablation (primary treatment). Long-term follow-up (renal/bladder function).

Cardiothoracic / Neuro

(Mark Allocation: 7 Marks)

Q: Tension Pneumothorax (CF, Mx). (SE)

Definition: Air enters pleural space, cannot exit -> pressure builds -> mediastinal shift -> cardiovascular collapse.

CF: Severe respiratory distress, Tachypnoea, Tachycardia, Hypotension, JVD, Tracheal deviation AWAY, Unilateral absent breath sounds, Hyperresonant percussion.

Diagnosis: CLINICAL. Do NOT wait for X-ray.

Management: IMMEDIATE Decompression.

  1. Needle Thoracocentesis: Large bore cannula (14-16G) in 2nd ICS MCL or 5th ICS AAL.
  2. Chest Tube (ICT) Insertion: Definitive treatment. 4th/5th ICS MAL ('Triangle of safety'). Underwater seal drain.
  3. Supportive: O2, IV fluids.
Q: Flail Chest (Causes, Mx). (SA)

Definition: ≥3 adjacent ribs fractured in ≥2 places -> detached segment.

Cause: Significant blunt trauma.

Pathophysiology: Paradoxical chest wall movement. Underlying pulmonary contusion is key problem -> hypoxia.

CF: Respiratory distress, pain, crepitus, visible paradoxical motion.

Mx: Airway/Breathing (O2, early PPV/Intubation often needed - internal splint). Pain control (Epidural ideal). Fluid balance (careful). Pulmonary toilet. Treat associated injuries.

Q: Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS). (SA)

Purpose: Assess level of consciousness objectively.

Components (Score):

  • Eye Opening (E): 4 Spontaneous, 3 To Speech, 2 To Pain, 1 None.
  • Verbal Response (V): 5 Oriented, 4 Confused, 3 Inappropriate words, 2 Incomprehensible sounds, 1 None.
  • Best Motor Response (M): 6 Obeys, 5 Localises pain, 4 Withdraws pain, 3 Flexion (Decorticate), 2 Extension (Decerebrate), 1 None.

Total Score: 3-15. Severity: Mild 13-15, Moderate 9-12, Severe ≤ 8 (Coma - intubate).

Q: Extradural Haematoma (EDH). (Focus for Pass / SA)

Cause: Trauma -> skull fracture (Temporal) -> Middle Meningeal Artery tear.

CF Classic: Trauma -> +/- LOC -> Lucid Interval -> Deterioration (headache, vomiting, ↓GCS). +/- Ipsilateral pupil dilation.

Ix: CT Head -> Biconvex / Lens-shaped collection.

Mx: Neurosurgical Emergency. Urgent Surgical Evacuation.

Q: Head Injury Management Principles. (SA)

Goal: Prevent secondary brain injury (hypoxia, hypotension, raised ICP).

Initial Mx (ATLS - ABCDE):

  • Airway with C-spine control (intubate if GCS ≤ 8).
  • Breathing (Oxygen, ventilation - avoid hyperventilation).
  • Circulation (Maintain SBP > 90-100 mmHg - crucial! Look for other bleeding sources).
  • Disability (GCS, pupils).
  • Exposure (Prevent hypothermia).

Ix: CT Head (Non-contrast) = Ix of choice. C-spine imaging. Bloods.

Specific Issues: Manage raised ICP (see below), surgical evacuation of mass lesions (EDH/SDH), seizure prophylaxis (Phenytoin 7 days in severe TBI).

Q: Subdural Haematoma (SDH). (SA)

Definition: Blood between dura and arachnoid.

Cause: Trauma (acceleration/deceleration) -> Tearing of Bridging Veins. Common in elderly/alcoholics.

Classification: Acute (<3d - hyperdense), Subacute (3d-3w - isodense), Chronic (>3w - hypodense).

CF: Acute (Often ↓GCS from injury, focal deficits). Chronic (Insidious - headache, confusion, drowsiness, fluctuating LOC).

Ix: CT Head (Non-contrast) - Crescent-shaped collection. Crosses sutures.

Mx: Acute (Surgical evacuation via Craniotomy if significant mass effect/deterioration). Chronic (Burr hole drainage if symptomatic).

Q: Raised ICP Management Principles. (SA)

Goal: ICP < 20 mmHg, CPP (MAP-ICP) > 60-70 mmHg.

Management (Tiered Approach):

  • Tier 0 (Basics): Head up 30°, midline position, normothermia, normoglycemia, sedation/analgesia, seizure prophylaxis.
  • Tier 1: CSF Drainage (via EVD), Osmotic therapy (Mannitol / Hypertonic Saline).
  • Tier 2: Optimize sedation, moderate hyperventilation (temporary), neuromuscular blockade.
  • Tier 3 (Refractory): Barbiturate coma, Decompressive craniectomy, Hypothermia (controversial).

Monitoring: Invasive ICP monitoring standard in severe TBI.

Male Reproductive System

(Mark Allocation: 6 Marks)

Q: Classify testicular tumours. Outline management of Seminoma testis. (SE)

Classification: Germ Cell Tumours (GCT >90%) vs Sex Cord-Stromal.

  • GCT: Seminoma (~50%) vs Non-Seminomatous GCT (NSGCT - Embryonal, Yolk Sac, Choriocarcinoma, Teratoma). Mixed common.

Seminoma Management (Staging key): (Markers: AFP always normal, β-hCG ~10% elevated, LDH reflects burden).

  • Stage I (Testis only): Radical inguinal orchidectomy. Adjuvant options: Surveillance (preferred low risk), Single-dose Carboplatin, or Radiotherapy (less used now due to long-term risks).
  • Stage II (Retroperitoneal nodes): Orchidectomy + Radiotherapy (IIA/small IIB) or Chemotherapy (BEP/EP - larger nodes).
  • Stage III (Distant mets): Orchidectomy + Chemotherapy (BEP). Consider PET/Surgery for residual mass >3cm post-chemo.

Note: Seminoma is highly radiosensitive and chemosensitive.

Q: Testicular Torsion. (Focus for Pass)

Focus:

  • Key Features: Sudden severe scrotal pain, nausea/vomiting, young age (<25 yrs), high-riding horizontal testis, absent cremasteric reflex.
  • Management principle: Urgent surgical exploration within 6 hours (detorsion + bilateral orchidopexy if viable; orchidectomy if non-viable).
  • Difference from epididymo-orchitis: Prehn's sign (pain relief on scrotal elevation) positive in epididymo-orchitis, negative/worse in torsion. Cremasteric reflex usually present in infection. Doppler USG can help if diagnosis uncertain but shouldn't delay surgery if high suspicion.

Why: Surgical emergency with time sensitivity. Common question.

Q: Congenital Hydrocele. (SA/SE)

Definition: Peritoneal fluid in tunica vaginalis via patent processus vaginalis (PPV).

Types: True Congenital (communicating - size varies), Infantile (PPV closed at top), Encysted (cord cyst).

CF: Painless scrotal swelling, transilluminant. Size may fluctuate (communicating). Getting above swelling possible.

Mx: Observe until 18-24 months (most resolve). Surgery (Inguinal approach - Herniotomy / high ligation of PPV) if persists, large, or associated hernia.

Q: Undescended Testis (Cryptorchidism). (SA)

Definition: Testis not in scrotum by 3-4 months.

Types: True Undescended (arrested path), Ectopic (deviated path), Retractile (hyperactive cremaster).

Complications (TESTIS): Torsion, Epididymo-orchitis, Sterility/Subfertility, Trauma, Indirect Hernia, Seminoma/Malignancy (increased risk).

Ix: Clinical exam. Laparoscopy = Gold standard for non-palpable.

Mx: Orchidopexy (surgery) = Treatment of choice, ideally at 6-12 months. Inguinal approach for palpable. Laparoscopy for non-palpable (diagnostic +/- therapeutic, may need staged Fowler-Stephens).

1. Hip Dislocation (Focus: Posterior Dislocation)

(Appears frequently: QP 1041 Mar-2024, QP 1096 Sep-2021, Dec-2017, Dec-2013)

Definition: Complete loss of contact between the articular surfaces of the femoral head and the acetabulum. Posterior dislocation is the most common type (approx. 90%).

Causes/Etiology: Primarily traumatic.

  • High-energy trauma (RTA, fall from height).
  • Mechanism: Force applied along the shaft of the femur with the hip in flexion, adduction, and slight internal rotation (e.g., dashboard injury where knee strikes dashboard).

Pathoanatomy:

  • Femoral head forced out of acetabulum posteriorly.
  • Posterior capsule ruptures.
  • Ligamentum teres tears.
  • Possible fracture of the posterior acetabular rim (fracture-dislocation).
  • Potential injury to the sciatic nerve (lies posterior to the hip).

Clinical Features (Signs & Symptoms):

  • History: Severe trauma (e.g., RTA).
  • Symptoms: Intense pain, inability to move the affected limb.
  • Signs:
    • Attitude (Classical): Hip held in Flexion, Adduction, and Internal Rotation (FAIR).
    • Limb appears shortened.
    • Head of femur may be palpable in the gluteal region.
    • Movements are painfully restricted.
    • Check Sciatic Nerve: Look for foot drop or sensory loss in its distribution.

Diagnosis:

  • Clinical: Based on history and characteristic limb attitude.
  • Radiological:
    • X-ray Pelvis with both hips (AP view): Confirms diagnosis. Shows femoral head superior and lateral to acetabulum. Lesser trochanter obscured due to internal rotation. Shenton's line broken. Look for associated acetabular rim fracture.
    • CT Scan: Useful to evaluate associated acetabular fracture size and comminution, check for intra-articular fragments.

Treatment:

  • Emergency: Prompt reduction is crucial to minimize risk of Avascular Necrosis (AVN).
  • Closed Reduction (under GA/sedation):
    • Allis' method: Patient supine, pelvis stabilized. Surgeon applies traction in line of deformity, gently flexes hip to 90°, applies upward traction with internal/external rotation to lever head back into acetabulum. Feel/hear a 'clunk'.
    • Stimson's method: Patient prone with leg hanging over table edge. Gentle downward pressure/rotation applied.
  • Post-reduction: Check stability, confirm reduction with X-ray, check neurovascular status (especially sciatic nerve). Skin traction for 2-3 weeks, followed by gradual mobilization (non-weight bearing initially).
  • Open Reduction Indications: Failed closed reduction, irreducible due to interposed tissue/fragment, large displaced acetabular fragment needing fixation, loose intra-articular bodies.

Complications:

  • Early: Sciatic nerve injury (10-15%), vascular injury (rare), associated fractures (acetabulum, femoral head/neck).
  • Late: Avascular Necrosis (AVN) of femoral head (up to 40%, risk increases with delay in reduction), Osteoarthritis (secondary to AVN or cartilage damage), Unreduced/Recurrent dislocation (rare), Myositis ossificans.
2. Fractures: Classification, Principles of Management, Healing & Complications

(Appears frequently: QP 1096 Jul-2023, Jun-2019)

Fracture Definition: A break in the continuity of a bone.

Classification: Can be based on:

  • Etiology: Traumatic, Pathological, Stress.
  • Displacement: Undisplaced, Displaced (Shift, Angulation, Rotation).
  • Relation to External Environment: Closed (simple), Open (compound - Graded by Gustilo-Anderson I, II, IIIA, IIIB, IIIC).
  • Pattern: Transverse, Oblique, Spiral, Comminuted, Segmental, Greenstick (children), Avulsion.
  • Location: Epiphyseal, Metaphyseal, Diaphyseal; Intra-articular vs. Extra-articular.
  • Stability: Stable vs. Unstable.

Fracture Healing (Secondary bone healing - typical for closed fractures):

  1. Stage 1: Haematoma Formation (First few days): Bleeding from bone ends & surrounding tissues forms haematoma. Inflammatory response begins. Fracture ends become ischaemic.
  2. Stage 2: Inflammation/Granulation Tissue (Up to 2-3 weeks): Haematoma organizes. Ingrowth of capillaries & fibroblasts. Granulation tissue forms, bridging the gap (soft callus). Osteoclasts remove dead bone.
  3. Stage 3: Callus Formation (4-12 weeks): Osteoblasts differentiate from precursor cells (periosteum, endosteum). Woven bone (immature) laid down within granulation tissue, bridging the fracture. Callus visible on X-ray around 3 weeks. Fracture becomes clinically stable (not rigidly).
  4. Stage 4: Consolidation/Remodelling (Months to years): Woven bone replaced by mature lamellar bone. Callus remodels according to Wolff's law (bone remodels in response to stress). Medullary canal reforms. Fracture site gradually becomes indistinguishable.

(Mention Primary Bone Healing: Occurs with rigid internal fixation, minimal gap, direct Haversian remodelling across fracture line, minimal external callus).

Factors Affecting Healing:

  • Age: Faster in children.
  • Type of fracture: Spiral > Oblique > Transverse. Open/Comminuted heal slower.
  • Blood Supply: Crucial. Poor supply (e.g., scaphoid waist, femoral neck, distal tibia) leads to delayed/non-union/AVN.
  • Immobilization: Inadequate immobilization delays healing.
  • Site: Cancellous bone heals faster than cortical. Metaphyseal faster than diaphyseal.
  • General factors: Nutrition, hormones, smoking, associated diseases (diabetes).
  • Local factors: Infection, soft tissue interposition, distraction, severe soft tissue damage.

Principles of Fracture Management (R-I-P):

  • Reduction: Restore acceptable alignment.
    • Closed: Manipulation, Traction.
    • Open: Surgical exposure and reduction (ORIF). Indications for ORIF: Failed closed reduction, displaced intra-articular fractures, unstable fractures, multiple fractures, pathological fractures, associated vascular injury.
  • Immobilization (Holding Reduction): Maintain reduction until union.
    • Non-operative: POP cast, slab, functional brace, traction (skin/skeletal).
    • Operative (Internal Fixation): Screws, plates, intramedullary nails, K-wires, tension band wiring.
    • Operative (External Fixation): Pins/wires connected to external frame (for open fractures, comminuted fractures, bone transport).
  • Preservation of Function (Rehabilitation): Maintain function of rest of the limb during immobilization & restore function of injured part after union. Static exercises in cast, active movement of adjacent joints, physiotherapy after cast removal.

Complications of Fractures:

  • Immediate (At time of injury): Hypovolemic shock, Injury to major vessels/nerves/viscera, Skin/muscle damage.
  • Early (First few days/weeks): Compartment syndrome, Fat embolism syndrome, DVT/Pulmonary embolism, Infection (esp. open fractures), Plaster sores, Nerve pressure palsies.
  • Late (Months/Years):
    • Union Problems: Delayed union, Non-union (Hypertrophic/Atrophic), Malunion (Angulation, Rotation, Shortening).
    • Avascular Necrosis (AVN): (e.g., Femoral head, scaphoid proximal pole, talus body).
    • Joint Problems: Stiffness, Post-traumatic osteoarthritis.
    • Muscle Problems: Volkmann's ischaemic contracture, Myositis ossificans.
    • Nerve Problems: Late nerve palsy (callus entrapment).
    • Other: Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS/Sudeck's atrophy), Growth disturbance (children - epiphyseal injury).
3. Osteomyelitis (Acute & Chronic)

(Appears frequently: QP 1096 Aug-2022, Oct-2020, Dec-2013; QP 1041 Jun-2024)

Definition: Inflammation/infection of bone and bone marrow. Can be acute or chronic. Pyogenic bacteria (esp. Staphylococcus aureus) are commonest cause.

Acute Haematogenous Osteomyelitis:

  • Etiology: Blood-borne spread of bacteria from a distant focus (often minor skin/throat infection). Commonest in children (boys > girls), long bones. Metaphysis is the usual site (due to sluggish blood flow in sinusoidal veins & sharp loops of nutrient artery end-branches). Common sites: Distal femur, proximal tibia, proximal humerus.
  • Pathogenesis: Bacteria lodge in metaphysis -> Inflammatory response -> Pus formation -> Increased intraosseous pressure -> Impaired blood supply -> Bone necrosis (Sequestrum formation). Pus spreads:
    • Along medullary cavity.
    • Through Volkmann's canals to subperiosteal space -> Periosteal elevation -> New bone formation under periosteum (Involucrum).
    • Into adjacent joint (if metaphysis is intra-articular, e.g., hip).
    • Through skin -> Sinus formation.
  • Clinical Features: Acute onset, high fever, chills, malaise, severe localized bone pain (throbbing), tenderness, swelling, redness, warmth over affected metaphysis. Child refuses to move limb (pseudoparalysis). +/- Joint effusion.
  • Investigations:
    • Blood: High WBC (neutrophilia), High ESR/CRP. Blood culture may be positive.
    • X-ray: Initially normal (may show soft tissue swelling). Periosteal reaction visible after 10-14 days. Lytic areas/sequestra appear later (2-3 weeks).
    • Bone Scan (Tc-99m): Positive within 48-72 hrs (increased uptake).
    • MRI: Shows marrow edema/abscess early, most sensitive investigation.
    • Aspiration: Pus aspiration (guided if needed) for Gram stain & culture/sensitivity.
  • Treatment:
    • Early (within 48 hrs): Conservative. IV antibiotics (high dose, broad-spectrum initially e.g., Cloxacillin + Cephalosporin/Gentamicin, adjust based on C/S), Analgesia, IV fluids, Rest/Immobilization (splint). Monitor response (fever, pain, ESR/CRP). Continue IV Abx for 1-2 weeks, then oral for total 4-6 weeks.
    • Late (>48 hrs or no response): Surgical drainage (decompression). Drill holes in metaphysis, evacuate pus, debridement. Continue antibiotics.
  • Complications: Chronic osteomyelitis, Septic arthritis, Pathological fracture, Growth disturbance (if physis damaged), Septicemia.

Chronic Osteomyelitis:

  • Etiology: Sequel to inadequately treated acute osteomyelitis, or direct infection (open fracture, surgery). Characterized by persistence of infection, presence of dead bone (sequestrum), infected granulation tissue, surrounding sclerotic bone (involucrum), and often discharging sinus(es).
  • Pathology: Presence of sequestrum (avascular dead bone, acts as foreign body harbouring bacteria), surrounded by infected granulation tissue & pus within a cavity lined by sclerotic bone (involucrum). Cloacae (openings in involucrum) allow pus drainage -> Sinus tract -> Skin opening.
  • Clinical Features: Persistent/recurrent discharge from sinus, Localized pain/tenderness (esp. during flare-ups), Thickened, irregular bone, Adherent skin/scars. +/- Systemic features during acute exacerbations.
  • Investigations:
    • X-ray: Shows thickened, sclerotic irregular bone, +/- sequestrum (denser than surrounding bone), cavity, involucrum, cloacae.
    • Sinogram: Inject contrast into sinus to delineate tract & extent of cavity.
    • CT/MRI: Better defines sequestrum, cavity, soft tissue involvement.
    • Biopsy/Culture: From sinus tract / operative sample to identify organism. Rule out malignancy (Marjolin's ulcer).
  • Treatment: Primarily surgical. Antibiotics used mainly for acute flare-ups or peri-operatively.
    • Sequestrectomy: Removal of sequestrum.
    • Saucerization: Unroofing bone cavities to allow drainage.
    • Excision: Resection of infected bone segment (if feasible, e.g., fibula). May need reconstruction (bone graft, Ilizarov).
    • Wound management: Packing, irrigation, flaps, skin grafting. Dead space management (muscle flaps, bone graft, antibiotic beads).
  • Complications: Recurrence, Pathological fracture, Amyloidosis, Squamous cell carcinoma in chronic sinus (Marjolin's ulcer), Joint stiffness/deformity, Limb length discrepancy.
4. Fracture Shaft of Femur

(Appears frequently: QP 1096 Aug-2024)

Introduction: Common fracture, usually due to high-energy trauma (RTA, fall from height) in adults, less force in elderly (osteoporosis) or children. Significant blood loss (1-1.5 L) can occur -> risk of hypovolemic shock.

Anatomy: Strongest bone, surrounded by powerful muscles (quadriceps, hamstrings, adductors). Displacement influenced by muscle pull. Proximal fragment: flexed (iliopsoas), abducted (glutei), externally rotated (short external rotators). Distal fragment: adducted (adductors), pulled proximally (hamstrings, quads), may be flexed (gastrocnemius if distal).

Classification:

  • Location: Proximal third, Middle third, Distal third.
  • Pattern: Transverse, Oblique, Spiral, Comminuted, Segmental.
  • Winquist Classification (Comminution): Type I (minimal), II (<50% cortical contact lost), III (>50% lost), IV (segmental, no contact).
  • Open/Closed.

Clinical Features:

  • History: Significant trauma.
  • Symptoms: Severe pain, inability to move/bear weight, swelling, deformity.
  • Signs: Obvious swelling & deformity (shortening, external rotation, angulation), tenderness, abnormal mobility, crepitus. Check distal neurovascular status (pulses, sensation, motor function). Look for associated injuries (hip, knee, pelvis, head, chest).

Radiological Features:

  • X-ray: AP and Lateral views of entire femur including hip and knee joints. Assess fracture location, pattern, comminution, displacement. Look for associated hip/knee injuries.

Management:

  • Emergency: ATLS principles. Control hemorrhage, IV fluids, analgesia. Immobilize limb (Thomas splint) to control pain & prevent further damage.
  • Definitive Treatment:
    • Children:
      • < 6 months: Pavlik harness.
      • 6 mths - 5 yrs: Immediate hip spica cast.
      • 5-11 yrs: Flexible intramedullary nails (TENS - Titanium Elastic Nail System) or external fixation / spica.
      • > 11 yrs: Consider adult methods (rigid IM nail).
    • Adults: Operative treatment is standard for early mobilization & better outcomes.
      • Intramedullary Nailing (IMN): Gold standard. Antegrade (entry via piriform fossa/greater trochanter) or retrograde (entry via knee). Usually locked (screws proximally & distally) for rotational stability & length control, especially in comminuted/proximal/distal fractures.
      • Plating (ORIF): Reserved for specific situations (e.g., distal fractures extending into joint, peri-prosthetic fractures, failure of nailing, polytrauma where IMN contraindicated). Higher infection/non-union risk compared to nailing.
      • External Fixation: For severe open fractures (Grade IIIB/C), polytrauma with instability, temporary stabilization.
    • Conservative (Traction): Rarely used as definitive treatment in adults now due to complications of prolonged bed rest. Skeletal traction (upper tibial pin) in Thomas splint used for temporary stabilization before surgery or in specific non-operative cases.

Complications:

  • Early: Hypovolemic shock, Fat embolism, DVT/PE, Infection (open fracture/ORIF), Nerve/Vessel injury (rare), Compartment syndrome (rare).
  • Late: Delayed/Non-union, Malunion (shortening, angulation, rotation), Knee stiffness (quadriceps adhesions), Implant failure/migration, Refracture, Infection (late).
5. Club Foot (Congenital Talipes Equino Varus - CTEV)

(Appears frequently: QP 1096 Feb-2023, Feb-2022, Jun-2019)

Definition: Congenital deformity of the foot characterized by four components:

  • Cavus (Increased medial longitudinal arch)
  • Adductus (Forefoot deviated medially)
  • Varus (Heel inverted)
  • Equinus (Foot plantarflexed at ankle) - Remember CAVE

Etiology: Mostly Idiopathic (90%). Theories include:

  • Genetic factors: Higher incidence in families.
  • Intrauterine factors: Mechanical pressure (oligohydramnios), arrested fetal development.
  • Neuromuscular factors: Possible imbalance (unproven in idiopathic).
  • Associated conditions: Can occur secondary to spina bifida, arthrogryposis etc. (Syndromic CTEV - often more rigid).

Pathoanatomy: Complex disorder involving abnormal development and contracture of bones, joints, ligaments, muscles, and tendons, primarily on the postero-medial aspect of the foot and ankle.

  • Bones: Talus small, neck deviated medially & plantarward. Calcaneum small, inverted (varus). Navicular medially displaced/subluxated on talar head. Cuboid medially displaced.
  • Soft Tissues: Contracture/shortening of ligaments (posterior capsule, talo-fibular, calcaneo-fibular, deltoid, spring ligament), tendons (esp. Tibialis Posterior, Tendo Achillis, FDL, FHL), muscles (calf muscles underdeveloped).

Clinical Features:

  • Inspection: Characteristic CAVE deformity, foot appears small, heel small & high-riding, deep posterior & medial creases, calf muscle wasting (even at birth in severe cases). Severity varies (supple/postural vs. rigid/true). Check for associated anomalies (hip dysplasia, spina bifida).

Diagnosis: Primarily clinical, based on characteristic appearance at birth. X-rays are difficult to interpret in infants due to unossified bones, used mainly in older children to assess correction/residual deformity (Kite's angle - talo-calcaneal angle is reduced in AP & lateral views).

Management: Aim is to achieve a plantigrade, functional, painless, mobile foot. Early treatment is crucial.

  • Ponseti Method (Gold Standard - Serial Manipulation & Casting): Starts soon after birth. Weekly gentle manipulation correcting deformities sequentially (Cavus -> Adductus -> Varus) followed by above-knee casting holding correction. Equinus corrected last, often requiring percutaneous Tendo-Achilles tenotomy (TAT) around 6-8 weeks. Casting continues for 3-4 weeks post-TAT. Highly effective (>90% success).
  • Maintenance: Crucial to prevent recurrence. Foot Abduction Brace (FAB) / Dennis Browne splint worn full-time for 3 months, then only at night/naps until age 4-5 years.
  • Surgical Treatment (Reserved for failures/late presentation/syndromic):
    • Soft Tissue Release: Usually Postero-Medial Soft Tissue Release (PMSTR) for resistant deformity (age 6mths - 2yrs). Involves lengthening/release of contracted tendons, ligaments, capsules on postero-medial side.
    • Bony Procedures (Older children >4yrs): Tendon transfers (e.g., Tibialis Anterior transfer), Osteotomies (e.g., Dwyer's calcaneal osteotomy for residual varus), Arthrodesis (e.g., Triple arthrodesis for rigid deformity in adolescents >10-12 yrs).

Complications: Recurrence (most common, often due to non-compliance with bracing), Residual deformity, Stiffness, Overcorrection (rocker-bottom foot).

6. Shoulder Dislocation

(Appears frequently: QP 1096 Mar-2021, Jul-2015)

Introduction: Most commonly dislocated major joint due to inherent instability (large humeral head, small shallow glenoid). Anterior dislocation is most common (>95%).

Anterior Dislocation:

  • Mechanism: Typically indirect force - fall on outstretched hand with arm abducted, extended, and externally rotated. Direct blow to posterior shoulder possible.
  • Types (based on head position relative to glenoid): Subcoracoid (most common), Preglenoid, Subglenoid, Subclavicular (rare).
  • Pathoanatomy: Humeral head displaced anteriorly and inferiorly. Often associated with:
    • Bankart Lesion: Avulsion of the antero-inferior glenoid labrum +/- bony rim fragment. (Essential lesion for recurrent instability).
    • Hill-Sachs Lesion: Compression fracture/defect on the postero-lateral aspect of the humeral head (impaction against anterior glenoid rim).
    • Capsular stretching/tear, rotator cuff tear (esp. older patients).
  • Clinical Features: History of trauma, severe pain, inability to move shoulder. Arm held slightly abducted and externally rotated, supported by other hand. Loss of normal rounded shoulder contour (flattening), acromion prominent. Humeral head palpable anteriorly (subcoracoid). Positive Dugas' test (unable to touch opposite shoulder). Check Axillary Nerve: Sensation over regimental badge area, deltoid contraction.
  • Diagnosis: Clinical suspicion high. X-rays (AP in internal/external rotation, Axillary lateral or West Point view) confirm diagnosis, show position of head, rule out fractures (glenoid rim, greater tuberosity), identify Hill-Sachs lesion.
  • Treatment:
    • Acute: Prompt closed reduction (under sedation/analgesia/GA) to relieve pain and prevent complications. Methods: Kocher's, Hippocratic, Stimson's, Milch. Post-reduction X-ray confirmation. Immobilize in sling (arm adducted, internally rotated) for 3 weeks (younger patients) or less (older patients). Followed by physiotherapy (pendular exercises -> active assisted -> active -> strengthening).
    • Recurrent Dislocation: Common, especially in young (<20 yrs) after first dislocation. Due to failure of healing of Bankart lesion/capsular laxity. Treatment is usually surgical stabilization (Bankart repair - open or arthroscopic, +/- capsular shift/plication).
  • Complications: Axillary nerve injury (most common nerve injury), Rotator cuff tear, Associated fractures (greater tuberosity, glenoid), Recurrent instability, Stiffness (Frozen shoulder), Vascular injury (rare).

Posterior Dislocation:

  • Less common (2-4%).
  • Mechanism: Indirect force (fall on flexed, adducted, internally rotated arm), direct anterior blow, or violent muscle contraction (seizure, electric shock).
  • Clinical Features: Often missed. Arm held adducted and internally rotated. Limited external rotation and abduction. Flattening of anterior shoulder, coracoid prominent. Head palpable posteriorly.
  • Diagnosis: High index of suspicion needed. X-rays: AP view may look normal ('light bulb' sign - humerus in internal rotation), Axillary lateral view is diagnostic.
  • Treatment: Closed reduction (traction, gentle external rotation). Open reduction if delayed/irreducible. Immobilization in neutral/slight external rotation.

Inferior Dislocation (Luxatio Erecta): Rare. Arm held fixed above head. Needs reduction.

1. Colles' Fracture

(QP 1041 Mar-2024, QP 1096 Feb-2022, Sep-2021, Aug-2022)

Definition: Fracture of the distal radius (within 2.5 cm of wrist joint), with characteristic dorsal displacement and dorsal tilt of the distal fragment. Commonest fracture in adults, especially elderly women (osteoporosis).

Mechanism: Fall onto an outstretched hand (FOOSH) with wrist in dorsiflexion.

Pathoanatomy/Displacements (6 components):

  1. Dorsal displacement (shift)
  2. Dorsal tilt
  3. Lateral displacement (radial shift)
  4. Lateral tilt (radial tilt)
  5. Supination
  6. Impaction (shortening)

Associated Injuries: Ulnar styloid fracture (common), TFCC tear, Scapholunate ligament injury, Median nerve compression (acute or late).

Clinical Features: History of FOOSH, wrist pain, swelling, deformity. Classical "Dinner Fork" deformity. Tenderness over distal radius, loss of function. Check neurovascular status (esp. Median nerve).

Radiological Features: X-ray wrist (AP & Lat). Confirm fracture, assess displacements (dorsal tilt on lateral view, loss of radial inclination & radial shortening on AP view). Look for associated ulnar styloid fracture, joint incongruity.

Treatment:

  • Undisplaced: Below-elbow POP cast for 4-6 weeks.
  • Displaced: Closed reduction (under haematoma block/sedation/GA) - disimpact -> traction -> palmar flexion & ulnar deviation. Immobilize in Colles' cast (below-elbow POP, slight palmar flexion, ulnar deviation, slight pronation) for 6 weeks. Check X-rays post-reduction and weekly for 2-3 weeks for redisplacement.
  • Unstable/Comminuted/Intra-articular/Redisplaced: Consider K-wire fixation, external fixation, or ORIF with plating.

Complications: Malunion (commonest - dinner fork deformity, radial shortening -> wrist pain/weakness), Stiffness (fingers, wrist, shoulder - prevent with exercises), Median nerve compression (Carpal Tunnel Syndrome), Sudeck's atrophy (CRPS), Rupture of Extensor Pollicis Longus (EPL) tendon, Non-union (rare), Secondary OA.

2. Osteoclastoma (Giant Cell Tumour - GCT)

(QP 1041 Mar-2024, QP 1096 Jun-2016)

Definition: Locally aggressive (sometimes malignant) primary bone tumour characterized by multinucleated giant cells in a stroma of mononuclear cells. Typically occurs after skeletal maturity.

Epidemiology: Age 20-40 years (after epiphyseal closure). Slight female preponderance.

Location: Epiphysis of long bones (extends to articular surface). Common sites: Distal femur, Proximal tibia (around knee ~50%), Distal radius, Proximal humerus. Sacrum also common.

Clinical Features: Insidious onset of pain and swelling near a joint. Pathological fracture may be the presenting feature. Local tenderness, palpable bony swelling (may have egg-shell crackling).

Radiological Features (X-ray):

  • Eccentrically located, expansile, lytic lesion in the epiphysis extending to the articular cartilage (but rarely crosses joint).
  • Well-defined margin, but no sclerotic rim (unlike simple bone cyst).
  • "Soap bubble" appearance (trabeculations within the lesion).
  • Cortex thinned, may be broken (pathological fracture).

Histology: Mononuclear stromal cells (the neoplastic component) and numerous multinucleated osteoclast-like giant cells (reactive). Grading (Jaffe) based on stromal cell atypia (Grade I - benign, II - locally aggressive/borderline, III - frankly malignant - rare).

Treatment: Aims to eradicate tumour while preserving function.

  • Intralesional Curettage: Standard treatment. Thorough removal of tumour tissue. Cavity often treated with adjuvants (phenol, liquid nitrogen cryotherapy, argon beam coagulation, high-speed burr) to reduce recurrence. Defect filled with bone graft or bone cement (PMMA - exothermic reaction kills residual cells, allows earlier weight-bearing, easier detection of recurrence).
  • Wide Resection (En bloc excision): For aggressive/recurrent tumours or expendable bones (e.g., distal ulna, proximal fibula). Requires reconstruction (arthrodesis, arthroplasty, large grafts).
  • Radiotherapy: For surgically inaccessible sites (e.g., spine, sacrum) or unresectable tumours. GCT is radio-resistant, requires high dose, risk of malignant transformation.
  • Medical: Denosumab (RANKL inhibitor) - used for unresectable/metastatic GCT, or pre-operatively to solidify lesion.

Prognosis: Recurrence rate after curettage ~15-50% (lower with adjuvants/cement). Metastases (usually lungs) occur in ~2-5% even with benign histology ('benign metastasizing GCT'). Malignant transformation is rare (primary or radiation-induced).

3. Foot Drop

(QP 1041 Mar-2024, QP 1096 Aug-2022, Mar-2021, Dec-2017)

Definition: Inability to actively dorsiflex the foot at the ankle joint, resulting in a high-stepping or foot-slapping gait.

Cause: Weakness or paralysis of the ankle dorsiflexor muscles (Tibialis anterior, Extensor digitorum longus, Extensor hallucis longus, Peroneus tertius). This is usually due to injury or dysfunction of the Common Peroneal Nerve or its branches (Deep Peroneal Nerve), or the L4/L5 nerve roots.

Etiology of Common Peroneal Nerve Palsy:

  • Trauma: Direct blow, laceration, fracture around knee (esp. fibular head/neck), tight cast/bandage, traction injury.
  • Compression: Habitual leg crossing, prolonged squatting, Baker's cyst, ganglion, tumour, external pressure during anaesthesia/coma.
  • Systemic: Diabetes mellitus, vasculitis, leprosy, polyneuropathy.
  • Iatrogenic: Surgery around the knee.

Clinical Features:

  • Gait: High-stepping gait (to clear the dropping foot) or foot slap (foot slaps down after heel strike). Patient may trip easily.
  • Motor: Weakness/inability to dorsiflex ankle & extend toes. Weakness of foot eversion may also be present (if superficial peroneal nerve involved). Test Tibialis anterior, EHL, EDL power.
  • Sensory: Loss/decrease over dorsum of foot and lateral aspect of lower leg (sparing medial malleolus - saphenous N; lateral border - sural N).
  • Tinel's sign: May be positive over nerve at site of injury/compression (e.g., fibular head).

Diagnosis: Based on clinical features. Differentiate LMN lesion (nerve palsy) from UMN lesion (stroke - associated features) or L4/L5 radiculopathy (back pain, SLR test, weakness of hip abductors/knee extensors may be present).

  • Investigations: Electromyography (EMG) and Nerve Conduction Studies (NCS) confirm nerve lesion, localize site, assess severity (axonotmesis vs neurapraxia), and monitor recovery. Imaging (X-ray, MRI) if fracture/tumour suspected.

Treatment:

  • Treat Underlying Cause: Remove compression, manage fracture, control diabetes etc.
  • Conservative: Physiotherapy (maintain joint mobility, prevent contractures, muscle strengthening if recovering). Ankle-Foot Orthosis (AFO) / Foot-drop splint to keep foot plantigrade during swing phase & prevent tripping.
  • Surgical:
    • Nerve Exploration/Repair/Grafting: For lacerations or failed recovery after expected period (axonotmesis).
    • Neurolysis: Release nerve from compression/entrapment.
    • Tendon Transfers (for permanent palsy): Reroute functioning tendons (e.g., Tibialis posterior transfer through interosseous membrane to dorsum) to restore active dorsiflexion. Requires muscle power grade 4/5 in donor tendon.
    • Arthrodesis: Ankle or triple arthrodesis for severe, fixed deformity (rarely needed).
4. Torticollis (Wry Neck)

(QP 1041 Mar-2024, QP 1096 Jun-2013)

Definition: Deformity characterized by tilting of the head towards one shoulder, with the chin rotated towards the opposite side.

Types/Etiology:

  • Congenital Muscular Torticollis (CMT): Most common type presenting in infancy/childhood.
    • Cause: Unclear. Theories include birth trauma (difficult delivery, breech), intrauterine malposition, vascular compromise leading to fibrosis of the Sternocleidomastoid (SCM) muscle.
    • Pathology: Fibrosis and shortening of one SCM muscle. May be associated with a "sternomastoid tumour" (firm, non-tender swelling in SCM) noticed in first few weeks of life, which usually resolves spontaneously.
  • Acquired Torticollis:
    • Ocular: Due to ocular muscle imbalance (e.g., superior oblique palsy), head tilt compensates for diplopia.
    • Osseous: Congenital vertebral anomalies (Klippel-Feil syndrome, hemivertebra), fractures, dislocations, infections (Grisel's syndrome - atlanto-axial rotatory subluxation secondary to pharyngeal infection).
    • Neurological: CNS lesions (tumours, dystonia), spasmodic torticollis (adult onset, involuntary muscle contractions).
    • Postural/Compensatory.
    • Inflammatory: Adenitis, pharyngitis causing muscle spasm.

Clinical Features (CMT):

  • Presents in infancy or early childhood.
  • Characteristic head tilt towards affected side, chin rotated away.
  • Tight, cord-like SCM muscle palpable on affected side.
  • Limited passive range of motion (tilting away from, rotation towards affected side).
  • Facial asymmetry (plagiocephaly) develops in untreated cases due to moulding.
  • May be associated with hip dysplasia (check hips!).

Diagnosis (CMT): Primarily clinical. Ultrasound can confirm SCM thickening/fibrosis, rule out tumour. X-rays of cervical spine to rule out bony abnormalities. Ophthalmic/Neurologic evaluation if acquired torticollis suspected.

Treatment (CMT):

  • Conservative (Majority): Started early (<1 year). Passive stretching exercises (gentle tilt away, rotation towards affected side), positioning (encourage looking towards affected side using toys/light). Effective in >90% if started early.
  • Surgical Release: Indicated if conservative treatment fails after 6-12 months, or presentation >1 year old with significant restriction/plagiocephaly. Involves release (tenotomy) or excision of the fibrotic SCM muscle, usually at its distal (clavicular/sternal) end(s), sometimes bipolar release. Post-op physiotherapy/collar needed.

Treatment (Acquired): Address the underlying cause.

5. Scaphoid Fracture

(QP 1096 Dec-2014)

Introduction: Most common carpal bone fracture. Typically occurs in young active males. Often missed initially. High risk of complications (non-union, AVN).

Mechanism: Fall onto outstretched hand (FOOSH) with wrist hyperextended and radially deviated.

Anatomy & Blood Supply: Waist (70-80% fractures) is narrowest part. Proximal pole (10-20%) and Tubercle (10%) fractures less common. Blood supply enters primarily distally and flows retrograde to supply proximal pole. Fracture at waist or proximal pole can disrupt this supply -> risk of AVN of proximal fragment.

Clinical Features: History of FOOSH. Pain & swelling in radial aspect of wrist ('anatomical snuffbox'). Tenderness specifically localized to anatomical snuffbox (between EPL and EPB/APL tendons). Pain on axial compression of thumb. Reduced grip strength.

Diagnosis:

  • Clinical suspicion is key. Treat as fracture even if initial X-rays negative.
  • Radiological:
    • X-ray: Standard wrist views (AP, Lat, Obl) PLUS dedicated 'Scaphoid views' (AP with ulnar deviation). May show fracture line (often subtle/hairline). If initial X-rays negative but suspicion high, immobilize and repeat X-rays in 10-14 days (fracture line becomes more visible due to resorption).
    • Bone Scan: Positive within 72 hours (increased uptake), sensitive but not specific.
    • CT Scan: Good for defining fracture pattern, displacement, assessing union.
    • MRI: Most sensitive & specific for detecting occult fracture & assessing vascularity (AVN).

Treatment: Depends on location and displacement.

  • Tubercle Fracture: Usually undisplaced, treated symptomatically (splint 2-4 wks).
  • Undisplaced Waist Fracture: Below-elbow thumb spica cast immobilization until union (typically 8-12 weeks, can be longer). High union rate.
  • Displaced Waist Fracture (>1mm) / Proximal Pole Fracture / Unstable Fracture: High risk of non-union/AVN. Usually require internal fixation (ORIF) with a compression screw (e.g., Herbert screw) - percutaneous or open. Allows earlier mobilization.
  • Non-union/AVN: Complex problem. Treatment options include bone grafting (vascularized/non-vascularized), internal fixation, radial styloidectomy, proximal row carpectomy, wrist arthrodesis depending on stage and symptoms.

Complications: Non-union (5-10%, higher in displaced/proximal pole), Avascular Necrosis (AVN) of proximal fragment (15-30% waist, near 100% proximal pole), Delayed union, Malunion, Scaphoid non-union advanced collapse (SNAC wrist) -> Osteoarthritis.

Plaster of Paris (POP)

Hydrated calcium sulfate (Gypsum). Used for immobilization. Hardens via exothermic reaction. Principles: Immobilize joint above & below, pad prominences, snug fit, functional position. Complications: Tight cast (compartment syndrome), sores, stiffness, nerve palsy.

Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (CTS)

Median nerve compression at wrist. Causes: Idiopathic, pregnancy, RA, hypothyroid, DM, Colles'. Symptoms: Pain/numbness/tingling in thumb, index, middle, radial half ring finger (worse at night, relieved by shaking). Thenar wasting late. Signs: +Tinel's, +Phalen's. Tx: Splint, NSAIDs, steroid injection, surgical decompression.

Brodie's Abscess

Chronic, localized osteomyelitis (intraosseous abscess). Usually metaphysis (tibia, femur) in young adults. Contains pus/granulation tissue, surrounded by sclerosis. Deep, boring pain (worse at night). X-ray: Lytic lesion, sclerotic rim. Tx: Curettage + antibiotics.

Sequestrum

Dead, avascular bone fragment separated in osteomyelitis. Acts as foreign body harbouring infection. Radiodense on X-ray. Requires surgical removal (sequestrectomy). Types: Tubular, Ring, Coke flake (TB), Sandy (TB), Feathery (Syphilis).

Seddon's Nerve Injury Classification

Neurapraxia: Conduction block, no axonal damage (myelin). Temporary. Full recovery. Axonotmesis: Axon damaged, sheath intact. Wallerian degeneration. Spontaneous recovery possible (1mm/day). Neurotmesis: Complete transection (axon + sheath). No spontaneous recovery. Needs surgical repair.

Pathological Fracture

Fracture through bone weakened by underlying disease (tumour, infection, metabolic disease, cyst). Occurs with minimal/no trauma. Healing depends on pathology. Tx: Manage fracture + underlying cause.

Night Cries

Characteristic of joint TB (hip/knee) in children. Pain during sleep when protective muscle spasm relaxes, causing child to cry out.

Mallet Finger

Flexion deformity of DIP joint due to extensor tendon avulsion/rupture at distal phalanx. Cause: Forced flexion of extended DIP (ball strike). Inability to actively extend DIP. Tx: Continuous DIP splinting in hyperextension 6-8 wks.

Hemiarthroplasty

Replacing only one articular surface (usually ball) with prosthesis (socket intact). Common for displaced femoral neck # in elderly (Austin Moore, Thompson). Contrast vs Total Joint Replacement.

Paget's Disease (Osteitis Deformans)

Chronic disorder of excessive, disorganized bone remodelling (↑osteoclast, ↑abnormal osteoblast). Elderly. Bones enlarged, deformed, brittle. Sites: Pelvis, femur, tibia, spine, skull. Complications: Pain, deformity, #, OA, nerve compression, high-output HF, osteosarcoma (1%). Tx: Bisphosphonates.

Skeletal Traction

Traction applied directly to bone via pins (Steinmann) or wires (Kirschner). Allows heavier weights vs skin traction. Used for femur, pelvis, cervical spine #. Requires pin site care.

Management of Rickets

Treat cause. Nutritional: Vit D (Stoss therapy or daily) + Calcium. Monitor healing (X-ray line in 2-4 wks). Correct deformities (osteotomy) once rickets healed. Other types: Treat renal/hepatic disease, phosphate for hypophosphatemic.

Cubitus Varus Deformity

Complication of supracondylar # malunion. Forearm deviates medially ("Gunstock deformity"). Cosmetic issue mainly. May cause tardy ulnar nerve palsy. Tx: Corrective supracondylar osteotomy if severe.

Ponseti Method

(See Club Foot). Sequential manipulation & casting for CTEV. Corrects C-A-V then E (often needs TAT). High success. Requires FAB/Dennis Browne bracing compliance.

Interlocking Nail

Intramedullary nail with proximal/distal holes for locking screws (through bone & nail). Provides axial & rotational stability. Standard for adult femur/tibia shaft #. Allows early weight bearing.

Volkmann's Ischemic Contracture

Late sequel of untreated compartment syndrome/vascular injury (e.g., supracondylar #). Ischemic necrosis of forearm flexors -> fibrosis -> Claw-like hand (wrist flexed, MP extended, IP flexed). Median/ulnar N damage common. Prevention key. Tx difficult.

Thomas Splint

Traction splint for femur # (temporary/definitive). Padded ring (groin) + side bars. Fixed or sliding traction. Requires careful application/nursing.

Shortwave Diathermy (SWD)

Physiotherapy deep heating modality (high-freq EM waves). Uses: Pain relief, reduce spasm, increase tissue extensibility. Contraindicated: Metal implants, malignancy, infection, ischemia.

Non-union

Fracture fails to heal in expected time (>6-9 mths). Types: Hypertrophic (biologically active, 'elephant foot', needs stability) vs. Atrophic (inactive, needs stability + graft). Causes: Poor immobilization/blood supply, infection etc. Tx: ORIF + graft, Ilizarov etc.

Trigger Thumb/Finger

Stenosing tenosynovitis of flexor sheath at A1 pulley (MP joint). Thickened pulley/tendon -> catching/locking in flexion, snaps on extension. Tx: Steroid injection, surgical release of A1 pulley.

Tennis Elbow (Lateral Epicondylitis)

Pain at lateral epicondyle due to overuse/inflammation of common extensor origin (esp. ECRB). Pain worse on resisted wrist extension/gripping. Tx: Rest, NSAIDs, physio, brace, steroid injection, surgery (resistant cases).

Anterior Drawer Test

Tests Anterior Cruciate Ligament (ACL). Knee flexed 90°, foot stabilized. Pull proximal tibia anteriorly. Excessive translation vs other side indicates ACL tear.

Popliteal Cyst (Baker's Cyst)

Synovial fluid in gastrocnemio-semimembranosus bursa behind knee, often communicates with joint. Usually secondary to OA, meniscus tear, RA. Swelling in popliteal fossa. Tx: Treat underlying cause, aspiration/steroid, excision if large/symptomatic.

Galeazzi Fracture

Fracture of distal third radius shaft + dislocation/subluxation of Distal Radio-Ulnar Joint (DRUJ). Unstable. Requires ORIF of radius; check DRUJ stability (may need pinning).

Complications of Septic Arthritis

Joint destruction -> Secondary OA, Ankylosis, Pathological dislocation, Osteomyelitis, Growth disturbance, Sinus formation, Septicemia. Early Dx & Tx (aspiration/washout, antibiotics) vital.

Rachitic Rosary

Beaded appearance of costo-chondral junctions in Rickets due to enlargement from uncalcified cartilage/osteoid.

Cold Abscess

Pus collection (caseous in TB) without acute inflammatory signs (heat, redness). Can track along fascial planes (e.g., psoas abscess from spinal TB).

Myositis Ossificans

Heterotopic ossification (bone in soft tissue) after trauma/surgery (esp. elbow, hip). Pain, swelling, progressive loss of motion. X-ray: Fluffy calcification -> mature bone mass. Tx: Rest, NSAIDs initially. Surgical excision only after maturation (6-12 mths) if functionally limiting. Avoid massage.

Bohler Brown Splint

Metal frame for elevation and skeletal traction for lower limb # (femur, tibia), esp. pre-op. Easier nursing/access vs Thomas splint skin traction.

Sudeck's Osteodystrophy (CRPS Type I)

Complex Regional Pain Syndrome. Severe pain (out of proportion), swelling, stiffness, skin changes (color, temp, sweating), patchy osteoporosis after minor trauma/surgery. Autonomic dysfunction. Tx: Multidisciplinary (physio, pain mgmt, psychotherapy). Early Dx important.

Ankle Injuries

Involves malleoli # (medial, lateral, posterior), ligament sprains (lateral commonest), syndesmotic injury. Lauge-Hansen classification. Tx based on stability: conservative if stable, ORIF if unstable (bimalleolar, trimalleolar, syndesmotic disruption).

Types of Sequestrum

(See Sequestrum SA). Dead bone in chronic osteomyelitis. Shapes: tubular, ring, coke flake, sandy, feathery.

Dequervain's Tenosynovitis

Stenosing tenosynovitis of 1st dorsal compartment (APL, EPB tendons). Pain over radial styloid, worse with thumb movement/ulnar deviation (+Finkelstein's test). Tx: Splint, NSAIDs, steroid injection, surgical release.

Osteochondroma (Exostosis)

Commonest benign bone tumour (developmental). Cartilage-capped bony outgrowth near metaphysis (esp. knee). Asymptomatic or pressure symptoms. Stops growing at maturity. Small risk (<1%) malignant change (chondrosarcoma). Tx: Excision if symptomatic.

Bennett's Fracture

Intra-articular fracture-dislocation of base of 1st metacarpal. Oblique # separates volar-ulnar fragment; rest of MC displaces radially/dorsally/proximally (APL pull). Needs anatomical reduction & fixation (K-wires).

Malunion

Fracture heals in unacceptable position (angulation, rotation, shortening, incongruity). Causes cosmetic/functional issues, secondary OA. Tx depends on severity: accept, corrective osteotomy, joint replacement.

Brown Tumour

Lytic bone lesion in hyperparathyroidism. Not true tumour; collection of osteoclasts, giant cells, fibrous tissue due to excess bone resorption. Reversible with treatment of hyperparathyroidism.

Flat Foot (Pes Planus)

Loss/flattening of medial longitudinal arch. Flexible (arch restores on tip-toe) vs Rigid. Common/physiological in children. Adult causes: Post Tib tendon dysfunction, laxity, arthritis. Flexible often asymptomatic (tx: supports if pain). Rigid/symptomatic acquired needs evaluation/treatment.

Ligaments of Knee Joint

Cruciates: ACL (prevents anterior tibial translation), PCL (prevents posterior tibial translation). Collaterals: MCL (resists valgus), LCL (resists varus). Also capsule, menisco-femoral etc.

Saturday Night Palsy

Radial nerve compression palsy in spiral groove (prolonged pressure). Causes wrist/finger drop, sensory loss over radial distribution distal to groove. Triceps spared. Usually neurapraxia, recovers spontaneously. Tx: Wrist drop splint, physio.

Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)

Chronic, systemic autoimmune inflammatory disease -> symmetric polyarthritis. Leads to joint destruction, deformity. Extra-articular features common. Tx: DMARDs, biologics, NSAIDs, steroids, physio, surgery (synovectomy, arthroplasty, arthrodesis).

Monteggia Fracture

Fracture proximal third ulna + dislocation of radial head (usually anterior). FOOSH/direct blow. Needs anatomical reduction ulna (ORIF plate) for radial head to reduce. Check PIN.

Acute Osteomyelitis

(See Osteomyelitis LE). Acute bone infection, usually haematogenous in children. Prompt Dx & Tx (antibiotics +/- drainage) vital.

Partial Claw Hand

Clawing (MP hyperextension, IP flexion) of ulnar two fingers (ring, little). Characteristic of ulnar nerve palsy (paralysis of ulnar intrinsics).

Fat Embolism

Serious complication post long bone/pelvic # (24-72 hrs). Fat globules enter circulation -> lungs (ARDS-like), brain (confusion), skin (petechiae). Gurd's criteria. Tx supportive (O2). Prevent: early fracture stabilization.

Triple Deformity of Knee

Seen in late knee joint destruction (TB, RA). 1. Flexion, 2. Posterior subluxation tibia, 3. External rotation tibia.

Osteoporosis

Low bone mass & microarchitectural deterioration -> increased fragility/fracture risk. Common: postmenopausal, senile. Dx: DEXA scan. Tx: Ca/Vit D, bisphosphonates, lifestyle.

Jaipur Foot

Low-cost, durable prosthetic foot (India). Allows squatting, walking barefoot. Vulcanized rubber exterior, wooden core.

Charcot's Joint (Neuropathic Arthropathy)

Destructive joint disease secondary to loss of sensation/proprioception (e.g., diabetes, syphilis). Repetitive microtrauma -> #, instability, degeneration. Swollen, deformed, unstable, often painless. Tx difficult: bracing, arthrodesis.

Psoas Abscess

Pus in psoas sheath, usually secondary TB spine. Hip flexion deformity (spasm), pain, fever, may point in groin/thigh. Dx: CT/MRI. Tx: Drainage + underlying cause (e.g., ATT).

Tuberculous Osteomyelitis

TB infection of bone (spine common - Pott's). Can affect long bone shafts (less common). Low-grade, lytic lesions, +/- sequestra ('kissing'), +/- cold abscess/sinus. Tx: ATT (9-12+ mths), +/- surgery.

Ainhum

Rare condition causing progressive constricting band around toe (usually 5th) -> auto-amputation. Cause unknown. Mainly African populations.

Fracture Clavicle

Common #, usually mid-shaft. Tx: Non-op (sling/figure-8) 3-6 wks usually. Surgery (plate) for open #, neurovascular compromise, severe displacement/skin tenting, symptomatic non-union, floating shoulder.

Involucrum

Sheath of new subperiosteal bone formed around sequestrum in chronic osteomyelitis. May have openings (cloacae).

Ganglion

Cystic swelling from joint capsule/tendon sheath (viscous mucinous fluid). Common: dorsum of wrist. Often asymptomatic. Tx: Observation, aspiration (recurrence common), surgical excision.

Spondylosis, Spondylolysis, Spondylolisthesis

Spondylosis: Degenerative changes (OA) spine. Spondylolysis: Pars interarticularis defect/fracture (often L5). Spondylolisthesis: Forward slip of vertebra (usually due to bilateral pars defect or facet degeneration). Tx depends on severity/symptoms.

Simple Bone Cyst (Unicameral Bone Cyst)

Benign fluid-filled cavity, metaphysis prox humerus/femur (children). Often presents with pathological # ('fallen leaf' sign). Tx: Observation, aspiration/steroid injection, curettage + graft (persistent/large).

Compartment Syndrome

↑Pressure in closed fascial compartment -> compromised perfusion -> ischemia/necrosis. Cause: # (tibia, forearm), crush, burns. Signs: Pain out of proportion, Pain on passive stretch, Paresthesia, Pallor, Pulselessness (late), Paralysis. Dx: Clinical, pressure measurement. Tx: Emergency Fasciotomy.

Montteggia Fracture Dislocation

(See above). Ulna # + Radial head dislocation. Bado classification. ORIF ulna essential.

Synovial Fluid

Normal: clear, viscous, straw-colored, <200 WBC. Analysis: Inflammatory (cloudy, low viscosity, ↑WBC, +/-crystals), Septic (purulent, low viscosity, ↑↑WBC >50k, +Gram/culture), Non-inflammatory (OA - clear, high viscosity, low WBC).

Marble Bone Disease (Osteopetrosis)

Rare genetic disorder, defective osteoclasts -> impaired resorption -> dense, sclerotic, brittle bone. Causes #, nerve compression, bone marrow failure. Tx supportive.

Skin Traction

Traction via adhesive straps/bandages to skin. Lighter weights (<5kg). Used temporary immobilization (hip # pre-op), definitive in children (Gallow's). Risk: skin issues.

Ewing's Tumor

Malignant small round blue cell tumour of bone (diaphysis long bones, children/adolescents 5-25). Pain, swelling, fever (mimics infection). X-ray: Lytic, periosteal reaction ('onion peel'/'sunburst'), soft tissue mass. Tx: Chemo + local control (surgery/RT).

Congenital Dislocation of Hip (DDH)

(See LE/SE). Spectrum: instability -> subluxation -> dislocation. Screening (Barlow/Ortolani, US). Tx: Pavlik harness, reduction + spica, osteotomies.

Orthoscopy (Arthroscopy)

(See LE/SE). MIS technique using camera (arthroscope) + small instruments via portals to visualize & treat intra-articular pathology.

K-wire (Kirschner wire)

Thin, smooth/threaded metallic wire for temporary/definitive fixation of small bone #, osteotomies, holding reductions. Often removed.

Spondylolisthesis

(See above). Forward slip of vertebra.

Exostosis (Osteochondroma)

(See above). Cartilage-capped bony outgrowth.

Kyphosis

Increased posterior curvature spine (usually thoracic). Causes: Postural, congenital, Scheuermann's, TB (angular/gibbus), osteoporosis, ankylosing spondylitis.

Osteopetrosis

(See above). Marble bone disease.

Above Knee Amputation

Amputation through femur. Stump management & prosthetic fitting important for rehab.

Claw Hand

Intrinsic minus hand (MP hyperextension, IP flexion). Due to paralysis of intrinsics (lumbricals, interossei - median/ulnar N). Can be partial (ulnar) or complete.

Cervical Rib

Extra rib from C7 vertebra. Often asymptomatic. Can cause Thoracic Outlet Syndrome (TOS) by compressing brachial plexus/subclavian artery. Tx: Conservative, surgical excision if severe.

Triple Deformity Knee

(See above). Flexion, posterior subluxation, external rotation.

Bennett's Fracture

(See above). Intra-articular #-dislocation base 1st MC.

Smiths Fracture

Reverse Colles'. Distal radius # with volar displacement & tilt. Mechanism: Fall on back of hand. Tx: Reduce, cast in dorsiflexion/supination.

Equines Deformity Ankle

Plantarflexion contracture of ankle. Cannot dorsiflex to neutral. Causes: Congenital (CTEV), spasticity, dorsiflexor paralysis, tight tendoachilles. Tx depends on cause.

Prosthesis

Artificial device replacing missing body part (limb, joint).

Osteomalacia

Defective mineralization of osteoid (adult rickets). Soft bones, pain, #. Cause: Vit D deficiency usually. X-ray: Looser's zones, osteopenia. Labs: ↓/N Ca, ↓PO4, ↑Alk Phos, ↓Vit D. Tx: Vit D + Ca.

Classification of Bone Tumor

Based on tissue origin, behaviour (benign/malignant), grade. Staging (Enneking/MSTS) considers grade, site, metastases.

Frozen Shoulder (Adhesive Capsulitis)

Idiopathic progressive pain & global stiffness of glenohumeral joint (capsular inflammation/contracture). Freezing -> frozen -> thawing phases (1-3 yrs). Tx: Analgesia, physio (gentle ROM), steroid injection, hydrodilatation, MUA, capsular release.

Bursae around the knee

Fluid sacs reducing friction. Common problems: Prepatellar (housemaid's), Infrapatellar (clergyman's), Pes anserinus, Semimembranosus. Bursitis from trauma/overuse/infection.

Gout

Metabolic disorder -> hyperuricemia -> MSU crystal deposition -> recurrent acute arthritis (esp 1st MTP - podagra), tophi, nephropathy. Tx Acute: NSAIDs, colchicine, steroids. Chronic: Allopurinol/Febuxostat, Probenecid.

Compound Fractures (Open Fractures)

Fracture communicates with exterior. High infection risk. Tx: Urgent debridement/irrigation, antibiotics, tetanus prophylaxis, stabilization (often external fix), wound closure/coverage. Gustilo-Anderson classification.

Barton's fracture

Intra-articular # distal radius + radio-carpal subluxation/dislocation. Fragment dorsal or volar (more common). Unstable, needs ORIF (buttress plate).

Crutches

Walking aids to reduce/eliminate weight bearing. Types: axillary, forearm/elbow. Need proper fitting & gait training.
